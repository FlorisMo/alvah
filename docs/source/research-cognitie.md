# Cognition, self-control, and PKU: What the evidence actually supports

**Three domains that once promised easy wins for child cognition have all been humbled by rigorous replication.** Sapropterin (Kuvan) lowers phenylalanine reliably in responders but delivers only modest, uncertain cognitive gains. Ego depletion—once textbook science—has collapsed under preregistered multi-lab replications. Executive-function training produces near-transfer to trained tasks but almost no far-transfer to real-world cognition. The common lesson: biology and behavior both resist simple leverage, and the strongest-looking original findings were often the most inflated. What survives is a narrower, honest toolkit in which **the biggest cognitive gains still come from early metabolic control, not from add-on drugs or brain-training apps.**

## Sapropterin lowers phenylalanine, but cognitive payoff is smaller than hoped

Sapropterin dihydrochloride is synthetic 6R-tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4), the cofactor for phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH). In the **20–50% of PKU patients with residual PAH activity**, extra BH4 boosts enzyme function and reduces blood phenylalanine (Phe). Dutch and EU guidance defines a responder as a patient showing ≥30% Phe reduction on a loading test at 10–20 mg/kg/day; patients with two "null" (0/0) mutations are exempted from testing because they lack the residual enzyme needed for the drug to work. The starting dose is 10 mg/kg once daily, titrated to 5–20 mg/kg; in BH4-deficiency the daily dose is split over 2–3 administrations.

The real clinical benefit in responders is **expanded natural-protein tolerance rather than dramatic IQ gains.** A 2024 two-year prospective UK cohort (BCH) reported responsive children tripling natural protein intake from about 10 g to 28 g/day, with 52% able to introduce cheese, eggs, meat, or fish once tolerance crossed ~25 g/day; non-responders showed no such change. The SPARK Phase IIIb trial (children under 4) and its 3-year extension confirmed safety and better Phe tolerance. The **7-year KOGNITO/US follow-up found FSIQ changes within ±5 IQ points** (least-squares mean change at year 7 = 1.14, lower 95% CI −3.53)—a preservation signal rather than a cognitive boost. A pretreatment neuroimaging study found responders already had **better white-matter integrity and higher IQ than non-responders before any drug exposure**, suggesting sapropterin responsiveness is a marker of milder disease, not a cause of better brains.

Caveat: sapropterin does not substitute for diet. Patients must continue Phe restriction and regular monitoring; hypophenylalaninemia, seizures (especially in BH4-deficiency), rebound on withdrawal, and interactions with levodopa and dihydrofolate-reductase inhibitors (methotrexate, trimethoprim) are documented. **Sephience (sepiapterin), FDA-approved in 2025 for ages ≥1 month, rescues roughly 43% of sapropterin non-responders**, widening the pharmacological net but not eliminating the central role of diet.

## Ego depletion: a textbook case of a theory that collapsed

The 1998 Baumeister, Bratslavsky, Muraven & Tice experiments—radishes vs. chocolate, counter-attitudinal speech—launched ego depletion: the claim that self-control draws on a limited resource that fatigues with use. By 2010, Hagger and Chatzisarantis's meta-analysis of 198 tests reported a medium effect (d ≈ 0.6), and roughly **600 studies over two decades appeared to confirm the phenomenon**, culminating in Baumeister's popular book *Willpower*.

Then the evidence eroded. Carter & McCullough's 2015 meta-analysis, correcting for publication bias via PET-PEESE, pushed the estimate toward d ≈ 0.2 and non-significance. **A 2016 preregistered 23-lab Registered Replication Report (n = 2,141) found essentially no depletion effect**, despite 22 of 23 labs predicting they would replicate it. A second, even larger multi-lab project led by Kathleen Vohs (36 labs, n = 3,531) likewise returned **d ≈ 0.06—an order of magnitude below the original meta-analytic estimate.** Michael Inzlicht, once a prize-winning ego-depletion researcher, publicly declared the effect a "mirage" after failing to replicate it in his own lab and has since argued that Baumeister's 2024 claim that ego depletion is "one of the most replicable findings in social psychology" is "wildly out of step with the evidence."

What survives is narrower. **Subjective mental fatigue is real**; the specific limited-resource "strength model" with glucose as fuel is not. Motivational reframings (Inzlicht & Schmeichel, 2012; Kurzban et al., 2013) recast depletion as a shift in *willingness* to exert effort, not ability. Critics (Vohs, Friese, Loschelder) further argue the revised model—with its "central governor" deciding when to conserve resources—has become **unfalsifiable because it can accommodate any data pattern**. The practical implication for clinicians and educators: do not design self-control interventions around the assumption that willpower is a fuel tank you can top up with glucose or protect by avoiding earlier exertion.

## Executive-function training: near-transfer yes, far-transfer barely

Children's EFs—working memory, inhibitory control, cognitive flexibility—can be trained, but **the gains rarely travel.** Kassai et al.'s 2019 meta-analysis of 90 experiments showed a solid near-transfer effect (g⁺ = 0.44, k = 43) for trained components, but **no convincing far-transfer** to untrained components (g⁺ = 0.11, k = 17, p = .11). Training working memory did not improve inhibitory control; training inhibition did not improve cognitive flexibility. Melby-Lervåg & Hulme (2013) reached the same verdict for working-memory programs: reliable task-specific improvement, no transfer to reasoning, reading, or arithmetic. Sala & Gobet (2017, 2020) found far-transfer effects near zero once active control groups were used (g ≈ 0.09 for academic outcomes), implicating **expectancy effects in studies using no-contact controls.**

The 2025 Birtwistle et al. meta-analysis is more optimistic about both near- and far-transfer, but the broader field remains cautious. Diamond and Ling's influential review argues the most durable gains come not from isolated computer drills but from **embedded, repeated-practice activities in daily life**—curricula like Tools of the Mind, martial arts, music, drama, and metacognitive strategy instruction—because they require the child to exert EFs across many contexts and at progressively harder levels. Neurofeedback, by contrast, shows symptom reduction in ADHD in early reviews but fails to move standardized EF tests in a 2022 meta-analysis.

| Intervention class | Near transfer | Far transfer to academics/daily life | Evidence quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Isolated WM computer training | Moderate (g ≈ 0.4) | Negligible (g ≈ 0.1) | Multiple meta-analyses converge |
| Multi-domain + metacognitive training | Moderate | Small but promising | Mixed, small samples |
| Embedded curricula (Tools, arts, sports) | Moderate | Small-moderate, broader | Diamond reviews; fewer RCTs |
| Neurofeedback for EF | Inconsistent | Null on standardized tests | Recent meta-analyses negative |

## A common thread: early bias inflated every headline number

All three literatures followed the same arc: **striking early effects → enthusiastic uptake → meta-analyses revealing publication bias → preregistered or large-cohort studies finding much smaller or null effects.** Ego depletion's d dropped from 0.6 to ~0.06. Working-memory far-transfer shrank from confident claims of fluid-intelligence gains to g ≈ 0.09 with active controls. Sapropterin's cognitive promise narrowed from "prevents intellectual impairment" to "preserves FSIQ within ±5 points while easing diet." The pattern indicates that **effect sizes in behavioral and pharmacological cognition research should be discounted heavily until replicated in preregistered, active-control, adequately powered designs.**

There is also an important asymmetry. Sapropterin's **biochemical endpoint (blood Phe) is hard, objective, and reliably moved**; its cognitive endpoint is softer and shows small effects at best. Ego-depletion's dependent variables (persistence on anagrams, Stroop performance) were always soft, and the underlying construct was underspecified—Saunders et al. (2018) showed different "self-control" tasks share only a small common core. EF training straddles both: trained tasks are hard measures, transfer outcomes are softer. **The harder and more proximal the outcome, the more the intervention holds up.**

## What this means for children and clinicians

For PKU, the evidence-based hierarchy is clear: **newborn-screening detection, immediate lifelong Phe-restricted diet starting in infancy, and tight metabolic control through age 12** account for the overwhelming majority of cognitive outcome. Sapropterin earns its place in BH4-responders mainly by enabling dietary liberalization and reducing caregiver burden; it should not be oversold as a cognitive enhancer. Pegvaliase (Palynziq, adults) and now sepiapterin (Sephience, 2025) extend options for non-responders. Dutch practice (Kinderformularium, Farmacotherapeutisch Kompas) mirrors EU guidance: test responsiveness with a loading dose, continue diet, monitor Phe and tyrosine 1–2 weeks after dose changes, and discontinue if Phe remains above target at 20 mg/kg/day.

For self-control and EF interventions in otherwise-healthy children, the honest message is that **brain-training apps do not buy you academic or behavioral gains**, glucose-replenishment strategies rooted in ego depletion are not supported, and the most defensible bets are embedded curricular approaches, metacognitive instruction, and physical/musical activities that repeatedly exercise EFs in rich contexts. Clinicians advising parents should **resist extrapolating from trained tasks to school performance** absent active-control evidence.

## Conclusion

The last decade rewrote three stories at once. Sapropterin is a real drug with real but modest effects, best understood as enabling dietary flexibility in responders rather than reversing cognitive impairment. Ego depletion, once psychology's flagship self-control theory, has been reduced to a motivational fatigue effect that survives only in loose, hard-to-falsify form. EF training produces exactly what you train and little more. **The unifying insight is that cognitive interventions—pharmacological or behavioral—rarely deliver broader downstream gains unless they act on the biological root (as early Phe control does for PKU) or are embedded in sustained, varied real-life practice.** Expect small effects, demand preregistered active-control evidence, and weight early metabolic and developmental windows over later add-on interventions.
