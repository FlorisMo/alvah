## 1. Verdict — what we build on

1. **Capacity ≠ deployment** (*zeker*). EF *capacity* (working memory, inhibition, flexibility —
   what the EF games already train) is largely lab-mature by mid-adolescence. The skill Floris wants
   — turning a vague urge into a *finished* self-chosen project — is EF *deployment*, gated by three
   later-maturing systems: **prospective memory**, **future orientation**, and **self-regulation**.
   A child can *hold* a plan in a test long before they will *use* one unprompted. **The game must
   supply the future-orientation the child's brain cannot yet supply for itself** (visible
   milestones, near-term done-states).
2. **Future orientation matures late** (*zeker*). Steinberg 2009 (N=935, 10–30): weak before ~16;
   planning-ahead keeps developing into the 20s. **So: long-horizon (months) goals are not
   developmentally *ownable* before ~16.** Build the spiral so the youngest rungs are one-step and
   the multi-month goal arrives last — never build a 9-year-old's "season project" as if he can hold
   it alone.
3. **A small set of tools is genuinely well-evidenced** (*zeker*): **implementation intentions**
   (if-then plans — works for under-12s, d≈0.43), **specific + proximal sub-goals**, **learning
   (process) goals over outcome goals for anything new**, **edge-of-ability feedback-rich practice**,
   and **GTD-style externalising of commitments** (capture, next-action, done-state). These are the
   spine. The flashy frameworks (eduScrum, full GTD) are weaker and older-only.
4. **The over-justification / Skinner-box risk is the central design tension** (*zeker*, **worse for
   children**). Deci/Koestner/Ryan 1999: expected tangible contingent rewards undermine free-choice
   intrinsic motivation (d = −0.40 / −0.36 / −0.28), while positive *informational feedback* enhances
   it (d = +0.33). **Points/badges/streaks are the single biggest threat to the very agency we are
   growing.** The default is NO tokens; the reward *is* the finished, visible artefact (§7).
5. **Graduation = contingent fading, not a schedule** (*zeker* on mechanism). Scaffolding that helps
   a novice becomes redundant or harmful once competent (expertise-reversal effect; Zimmerman's
   observation→emulation→self-control→self-regulation ladder). Hand off on *evidence of independence*,
   not age — but the developmental ceiling means piloting real tools around ~12–13 and real boards
   ~15–16 (§8).

## 2. What this trains, and why (design intent)

Turn a vague urge into a defined project — scope, milestones, a done-state — and actually finish;
set goals and hit them; and the meta-skill of **deliberate practice** (getting better at getting
better). The AI-era bet: when execution gets cheap, *deciding what to do and seeing it through* is
the part that stays human. Floris's own lean — sensing a **tension** and turning it into action
(distributed authority, not obedience-to-task) — is the framing, **but** the research is blunt that
there is *no* child-evidence for governance/Holacracy framing; we keep the *tension→action* spirit
(notice what bugs you → make it a project) and **drop the governance vocabulary entirely** for a
child. Diegetic fit is **diegetic-leaning**: restoration projects on the reserve are real projects
the ranger scopes, plans and ships; the meta layer (the board, the review) stays light.

## 3. The spiral 7→21

| Age band | Rung | What it actually is | Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7–8 | **One-step task** | Capture one "thing that bugs me," do one concrete action, see it done | Realistic. Long-horizon goals = push-too-early |
| 8–9 | **Multi-step plan (2–3 steps)** | A tiny project with a visible done-state; *the system holds the steps* | Realistic **with external scaffolding + near-term payoff** |
| 10–11 | **Milestone within a week** | One named checkpoint, reachable in days; first taste of "not done yet" | Realistic. Multi-week solo milestones still shaky |
| 11–13 | **Goal + target with proximal sub-goals** | A 2–4 week project: one-sentence story, definition of done, 2–5 milestones | Realistic **with reminders + a visible board**. Owning multi-month goals = push-too-early |
| 13–15 | **Parallel projects (2–3) under a WIP limit** | Juggling a few live projects; weekly review; learning goals | Emerging; **WIP limit essential**. Unlimited parallelism = push-too-early |
| 15–17 | **Long-horizon goal (months)** | Owning a multi-month outcome with self-set milestones | Becomes realistic ~16+ (future orientation matures) |
| 16–21 | **Real self-run project board + real tools/commitments** | Running own kanban/GTD-style system with real stakes | Realistic with *fading* support; full early handoff risks dependency-collapse or overwhelm |

## 4. Frameworks we'll use (evidence + veto status)

Veto column is Floris's (PLAN §7.1); blank = not yet ruled. ⭐ = spine centrepiece.

- **GTD-style capture / next-action / done-state** ⭐ — externalise every commitment; "if >1 step
  it's a project, define the next action." *zeker* for adults, *waarschijnlijk* for kids in stripped
  form. Kernels 9+, fuller 13+. **Trademark caution:** "GTD," "Mind Like Water," "Weekly Review" are
  registered marks — rewrite the workflow generically, never use the marks in-game (§9).
- **Scope→Done→Milestones** ⭐ — one-sentence story, minimum scope, binary checkable done, 2–5
  milestones with milestone-1 reachable in week one. *zeker* (proximal goals + done-state +
  commitment). 9+, scaling. *This is Floris's own spine and it is evidence-aligned.*
- **Implementation intentions (if-then)** — pre-decide when/where/how. *zeker* (d≈0.65 overall;
  ~0.43 for under-12s — genuinely works for young children). 8+.
- **Personal kanban (visualise + limit WIP)** — two rules; the WIP cap forces *finishing*.
  *waarschijnlijk* (strong rationale, light formal kid-evidence). 9+. Rewrite generically (§9).
- **Definition of Done** — a binary, checkable finish line. *zeker* (clarity principle). 8+.
- **Learning goals > outcome goals for novel tasks** — "learn to…" beats "achieve X" when the skill
  is new; outcome goals invite tunnel-vision + anxiety. *zeker*. 9+.
- **Deliberate-practice features** — edge-of-ability, immediate specific feedback, repetition with
  correction. *zeker* as *a* driver (not the whole story — see §8 honesty). 10+.
- **Self-Determination Theory (autonomy/competence/relatedness)** — the *design constitution*, not a
  mechanic. *zeker*. All ages. Anything that makes the child feel *controlled* (including rewards) is
  suspect.
- **eduScrum / scrum-for-kids** — sprints, stand-ups, retros. *waarschijnlijk/weak* (practitioner
  reports, thin controlled evidence). 13+, teams. **? [needs veto]** — likely overkill for solo play.
- **Project-based-learning "need to know" / Reggio-style emergent agency** — *waarschijnlijk*,
  fidelity-dependent. Light-touch flavour only, all ages.
- **✗ Holacracy/Sociocracy governance vocabulary for the child.** Floris's lean *informs* the spirit
  (tension→action), but there is no child-evidence for governance framing — keep "notice what bugs
  you → make it a project," drop roles/circles/proposals as kid-facing language.
- **✗ "10,000 hours" framing**, **✗ controlling reward loops** (§7), **✗ marshmallow-test "train
  willpower" framing** (§8).

## 5. Game mechanics to build

Almost entirely **Model B** (portfolio of finished projects + mastery rungs for planning moves). The
few Model-A-shaped pieces are the deliberate-practice ladder and calibration, which can plug the EF
staircase.

**Model B — mastery / portfolio (the core):**
- **Scope-the-Restoration** — cut a vague urge to minimum viable scope; one-sentence story ("As a
  ranger I want the pond edge clear so frogs return") → smallest version → binary done. *Mastery on
  tightness of scope.* Younger = pick from 2 pre-scoped options; older = scope from scratch & defend
  the cut.
- **Notice-and-Name (capture)** — wander the reserve, tap anything that "bugs you," it drops into an
  inbox, later clarify into project / reference / discard. *Track ratio of self-initiated captures
  that became finished projects* — never a point total. This is the diegetic home of the
  tension→action spirit.
- **Milestone-Marker** — split a restoration into 2–5 visible stakes along a trail; reaching one
  reveals the next vista. Younger = milestones pre-placed; older = child places them and must make
  milestone-1 reachable *today*.
- **Definition-of-Done Gate** — set the checkable done before starting ("10 saplings planted AND
  watered"); the gate won't open until done is literally true. *Mastery on "was your done actually
  checkable?"*
- **If-Then Ranger Cards** — attach an if-then to each project ("If it's raining, then I sort seeds
  indoors"); when the trigger fires in-world, the plan auto-surfaces. Younger = pick from a deck;
  older = author and rehearse. *Track % of triggered plans executed.*
- **WIP-Limited Toolshed** — the shed has only N tool-pegs; can't start a new restoration until you
  finish one and free a peg. *Throughput (finished ÷ started); a visible "Done meadow."*
- **Weekly Reserve Walk (reflect)** — a recurring guided walk past all live projects: mark progress,
  re-scope, retire stale ones. Younger = 3-question walk; older = full parallel-project review.
- **Before/After Lens / Reserve Almanac** — every finished restoration photographed before/after.
  **The almanac *is* the reward** — competence made visible, no points. Older learners annotate what
  they'd do differently.
- **The Itch Log** — a private log of "things that bug me about the reserve"; periodically the game
  asks "want to turn one into a project?" *Conversion rate itch→project→done.*
- **Re-scope Rescue** — a stalled project triggers "shrink the done-state to something finishable
  this week?" Younger = suggested smaller scope; older = must propose it. *Recoveries logged.*
- **Help-Fade Mentor** — an NPC ranger gives *less* help each time you succeed at a task type
  (contingent fading); eventually "you don't need me here." Fade rate tied to demonstrated
  independence; the "flying solo" marker **retires the mentor** (informational, not collectible).
- **Two-Minute Path** — tiny tasks (<2 min) flagged "just do it now"; doing them clears the trail
  visibly. The child learns to *judge* what's a two-minute job.
- **Season-Long Stewardship** — adopt a corner of the reserve for a whole in-game season with
  self-set milestones. **Unlock-gated to the ~15–16 maturity band** (future orientation). Season
  almanac + self-evaluation.

**Model A — scored (plug the EF staircase):**
- **Edge-of-Ability Trail (deliberate practice)** — a skill (e.g. dry-stone wall building) offers a
  rung one step harder than last success, with instant visual feedback per placement; difficulty
  **auto-tunes to ~70–85% success**. *Mastery ladder + personal-best portfolio, not scores.* This is
  the flagship — see §6.
- **Predict-Then-Check (calibration)** — before a task the child predicts "done by Friday?";
  afterward the game shows prediction vs. reality, gently. *Calibration accuracy over time.* **Shares
  the calibration spine with area 2** (see [01-logic-math-epistemics.md](01-logic-math-epistemics.md)
  §6) — reuse the Sure-O-Meter machinery, reskinned to effort/time estimation. Don't re-derive it.

**Build dependencies / unlock gates:**
- **Season-Long Stewardship** is unlock-gated to the late band (don't surface long-horizon ownership
  early — it produces abandoned goals, not agency).
- **Help-Fade Mentor** fades on *demonstrated independence*, not on a clock (§8 graduation signals).
- Several feedback-half mechanics (a coach critiquing a *finished* restoration, a stakeholder
  reacting to a proposal) depend on the **§4b AI-simulation engine** + its child-safety rails, and on
  the PLAN §7.6 privacy ruling. Mark them blocked until that lands.

## 6. Flagship deep-dive — deliberate practice that feels like play

The hard problem: deliberate practice (focused, edge-of-ability, feedback-rich) and play look like
opposites. The reconciliation the youth-sport literature supports is to embed deliberate-practice
*features inside* play-shaped loops, keeping the child's sense of choice intact. Five moves:

1. **Auto-tuned difficulty → ~70–85% success.** Always *almost* able (the flow zone); never a wall,
   never trivial. This is literally the EF adaptive staircase, reused.
2. **Immediate, specific, task-level feedback** — show *what* to adjust next attempt, not a grade
   (Hattie: task/process/self-regulation levels, not "self" praise).
3. **Repetition with variation** (interleaving — a *desirable difficulty*, Bjork) so it feels like
   exploring, not rote.
4. **Player-chosen targets** — the child picks which sub-skill to push (SDT autonomy).
5. **Visible personal-best trajectory** (mastery ladder/portfolio) instead of points — the reward is
   competence made visible.

**Guard against the fluency illusion:** smooth in-session performance is *not* learning. Use spacing
and delayed re-tests so readiness is judged on durable performance, not in-the-moment ease (Bjork &
Bjork). This is the same anti-cramming logic the spaced-rehearsal pieces use elsewhere.

## 7. Motivation design — repo-wide rules (binding on every area)

The mechanism is the over-justification effect via SDT's cognitive evaluation theory: rewards that
feel **controlling** ("do this to get that") undermine intrinsic motivation; rewards that feel
**informational** ("this marks genuine mastery") can support competence. Most gamification defaults
to controlling because it's the easiest thing to build (complete lesson → XP). The rules:

1. **Reward the artefact, not the activity.** The finished, visible restoration (before/after
   almanac) *is* the reward. Don't bolt detachable tokens onto intrinsically interesting work — the
   exact Lepper marker-study trap.
2. **No expected, tangible, contingent rewards for creative/agency tasks** — the most-undermining
   category (d = −0.28 to −0.40; worse for children). Acknowledgement *after the fact* that *informs
   competence* is least harmful (positive feedback was d = +0.33 in the same meta-analysis).
3. **Any "badge" is informational, not collectible** — it certifies genuine mastery ("you can scope
   a project solo") and ideally *retires a scaffold* rather than accumulating. **No leaderboards** for
   a single learner — they add nothing and can embarrass.
4. **No punishing streaks.** Streaks manufacture compulsion (Skinner-box) and turn a missed day into
   guilt. For continuity, show a *gentle progress map*, not a breakable chain.
5. **Protect autonomy at every choice point.** The child chooses projects, scope, and which skills to
   push. Framing is invitational ("want to…?"), never controlling ("you must…").
6. **Competence feedback must be honest.** Children are systematically over-confident; feedback
   should gently improve calibration (predict-then-check), not inflate it.
7. **Relatedness via the parent, not the app.** Shared review walks satisfy relatedness without the
   app manufacturing social pressure — lean on "sometimes Floris is present," don't simulate friends.

**This connects to the area-3 bright line:** the anti-gambling veto (no variable-ratio, near-miss,
loot-box) in [02-finance-and-value.md](02-finance-and-value.md) §6 and this anti-token default are
the *same* commitment from two angles — neither manufactured compulsion nor manufactured extrinsic
drive. Brief 6 (the meta brief) will fold both into the architecture-level reward rules.

## 8. Honesty lines — what we must NOT claim or do

- **Deliberate practice is *a* driver, not destiny.** "10,000 hours" is a Gladwell popularisation
  Ericsson disputed. Macnamara 2014: practice explained 26% of variance in games, 21% music, 18%
  sports, **4% education, <1% professions.** Teach the *mechanics* (tight feedback, edge-of-ability);
  never promise mastery is just a function of hours.
- **Don't build on the marshmallow test.** Watts/Duncan/Quan 2018 (N=918): an extra minute waited at
  age 4 predicted only ~0.1 SD at age 15, two-thirds of which vanished with background controls.
  Patience matters, far less deterministically than folklore. (Shared honesty line with
  [02-finance-and-value.md](02-finance-and-value.md) §8.)
- **Goal-setting theory is adult/workplace-origin and assigned-goal-heavy.** The child-ownership
  adaptation is theoretically sound but less directly evidenced (*waarschijnlijk*). SMART goals are
  *not* Locke & Latham and are widely cargo-culted — they say nothing about whether a goal is *worth*
  pursuing.
- **eduScrum / PBL evidence is weak/quasi-experimental** — engagement claims are mostly
  practitioner-reported; don't over-trust grade-gain figures.
- **No research targets a *solo* child on a tablet building self-directed projects over 14 years.**
  Almost all EF/SRL/goal research is classroom, lab, or workplace; transfer to *this* design is
  inference, not direct evidence (*waarschijnlijk*). Say so.
- **Handoff readiness checklists are heuristics, not validated thresholds** — the *mechanisms*
  (expertise-reversal, Zimmerman levels) are well-evidenced; the exact age boundaries are not.
- **"Over-scaffolding → learned helplessness" is plausible but mostly asserted**, not proven in
  controlled studies; the expertise-reversal *harm of un-faded guidance* is the well-evidenced half.

**Graduation signals (feed PLAN §7.4 — hand off more when these appear):** recognition AND production
independence (can both spot a good plan *and* produce one unprompted); self-initiated capture;
adaptive strategy use across *novel* projects (not just template emulation); longer independent
stretches between help-requests; durable performance on delayed/transfer checks; **and the child
*asking* for more autonomy** (a strong honest signal). Staged: sandbox+mentor (~7–12) → hybrid real
small project on a simplified real board, parent as reviewer (~12–14) → real tools, real commitments,
weekly review owned by the young person (~15–17). Pitfalls: too early → overwhelm + dependence-collapse
(future orientation isn't there pre-16); too late → expertise-reversal drag + lost ownership.

## 9. Accessibility (dyslexia + planning load)

Audio-first; never let the *reading* bottleneck become the *project* bottleneck. **Voice/photo
capture + text-to-speech everywhere** so the child can capture ideas and read instructions without
reading load gating the task. Icons + colour + audio carry planning information, not paragraphs.
Plain layout (clear sans-serif, ≥16px user-scalable, ~1.5× line spacing, left-aligned never
justified, ≥4.5:1 contrast, short lines, no body italics); treat OpenDyslexic/Dyslexie as *options
with mixed evidence* — rely on layout + audio, not a font. **Planning-load specifics:** one decision
at a time; pre-filled templates; **binary done-states (checkbox, not essay)**; the WIP limit doubles
as a cognitive-load limit. Avoid time-pressured reading, walls of instructions, and any reward system
that adds a *second thing to track*. (Consistent with the EF/area-1/2 a11y line: ≥56px targets,
dual-channel feedback, reduced-motion.)
