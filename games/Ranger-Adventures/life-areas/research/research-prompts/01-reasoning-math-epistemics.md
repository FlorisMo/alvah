## PASTE THIS INTO DEEP RESEARCH

**Context.** I am building a long-horizon educational game for my son (currently 8, Dutch, dyslexic,
strong reasoner) that he can keep playing for years — difficulty grows with skill. It already trains
executive functions; I am now adding broader life skills. This brief covers **reasoning/logic/math**
and **epistemics** (knowing how you know). My north star is the *AI era*: when a machine is fluent
and often confidently wrong, the durable human skills are valid reasoning and good calibration. I
dislike CBT and dislike praise-inflation; I want honest, evidence-grounded pedagogy, not hype.

**Audience for the research:** me (the parent/designer), so I can distil it into game mechanics and a
spiral curriculum spanning roughly **ages 7 → 21**. Assume a single learner, solo or with a parent,
on a tablet. No classroom, no grades.

**Research questions — go deep on each:**

1. **What is the actual developmental progression of reasoning, ages ~7 to ~21?** Map concrete
   rungs: from sorting/sequencing and if-then, through cause-vs-correlation, simple proof ("why must
   this be true"), conditional and probabilistic reasoning, to formal/abstract argument. Flag what is
   developmentally realistic at each age and what is commonly pushed too early.
2. **Math, specifically: number sense before procedure.** Summarise the strongest evidence on
   building deep number sense (concrete→pictorial→abstract; the Boaler / "mathematical mindsets"
   line; Singapore-style bar models). What sequences genuinely build understanding vs rote fluency?
   Where does fact-fluency (e.g. timed drills) actually help vs harm motivation, and how to do it
   humanely?
3. **Computational thinking** (decompose / pattern / abstract / algorithm) for kids — the CS-Unplugged
   tradition. What concretely transfers, what is overclaimed, and what unplugged activities are best
   evidenced?
4. **Critical thinking & informal logic for children:** argument mapping, identifying assumptions,
   common fallacies. Which fallacies are worth teaching a child and at what age? Is there evidence
   that explicit fallacy instruction transfers, or is it better taught implicitly through examples?
5. **Epistemics & calibration — the core AI-era ask.** What is known about teaching children to (a)
   rate their own confidence ("how sure, 1–5"), (b) update on evidence (intuitive Bayesian / base
   rates), (c) steelman before disagreeing, (d) judge a *source*. Is calibration trainable in kids?
   What measurement (calibration curves, Brier-style scoring) is age-appropriate and motivating
   rather than discouraging?
6. **Evaluating AI / machine output specifically.** Any emerging research or credible practitioner
   frameworks on teaching young people to assess AI answers, spot confident hallucination, and ask
   "how would I check this." This is the most important and likely least-researched part — flag the
   confidence level honestly.
7. **Probability as the bridge** to the finance brief: how do children best build intuitions for
   chance, expected value, and risk? What games/manipulatives are best evidenced?

**Honesty constraints (important):**
- Tag every substantive claim **zeker** (solid evidence) or **waarschijnlijk** (plausible / weak
  evidence). Separate "well-evidenced" from "popular but thin."
- Do **not** overclaim transfer. If "teaching logic makes kids reason better generally" is unproven,
  say so plainly. I would rather know the limits than be sold a program.
- No CBT-framed "challenge your irrational thoughts" material. This is reasoning, not therapy.

**Required output format (so I can build from it):**
- A **rung-by-rung spiral** for ages 7→21 for *reasoning*, *math*, and *epistemics* separately
  (a table per strand: age band · concept · what it actually is · realistic / push-too-early flag).
- A **framework menu** for each strand: name · one-line · evidence flag · best for which age · my
  veto column left blank.
- **10–15 concrete game-mechanic seeds** I could build (each: skill trained · core loop · how it
  scales with difficulty · which tracking model fits — scored/psychometric vs mastery).
- A **calibration-mechanic deep-dive**: exactly how to let a kid bet/rate confidence and get
  honest, non-discouraging feedback, with the scoring math.
- **Accessibility notes** for a dyslexic 8-year-old (reading load, audio-first, symbol vs text).
- An **open-gaps / caveats** section: what the evidence does *not* support, and what is my-judgement-
  call rather than established.
- **Sources**: prefer peer-reviewed and credible practitioner sources; cite them; note CC0/open
  materials I could legally draw from (clean-room — I rewrite everything, never lift).
