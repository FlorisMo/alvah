## PASTE THIS INTO DEEP RESEARCH

**Context.** I am building a long-horizon educational game for my son (currently 8, Dutch, dyslexic)
that grows with him for years. This brief covers two linked areas. **Creativity & making:**
generating ideas worth having and building them (art, music, stories, inventions, code) — and
developing *taste*, the ability to tell good from bad. **Ethics:** what is worth doing, what we owe
other people and creatures, how to weigh competing goods — as *judgement to develop*, not rules to
obey. My AI-era belief: when generation is cheap, original intent and taste become the scarce goods,
and agency without an ethical keel is just efficient optimisation of possibly-bad ends.

**Audience:** me (parent/designer), distilling into game mechanics and a spiral curriculum across
**ages 7 → 21**. Single learner, tablet, sometimes with a parent. The game has a natural ethics
sandbox: my son plays a wildlife ranger facing real dilemmas (intervene vs let nature be, visitor
access vs habitat protection, reporting a neighbour).

**Research questions — go deep:**

*Creativity & making:*
1. **Developmental progression of creativity, ages ~7 to ~21.** What is known about how creative
   ability develops (divergent thinking, the "fourth-grade slump," adolescent creative identity)?
   What helps vs what kills it?
2. **Divergent → convergent.** Best-evidenced practices for generating many options then selecting
   well; the role of constraints in provoking creativity; iteration and the "ugly first draft."
3. **Taste / critical appreciation as a trainable skill.** How do you teach a child to *evaluate*
   work (their own and others'), not just produce? This matters doubly in the AI era — judging AI
   output is the same muscle. What develops aesthetic/critical judgement?
4. **Feedback & praise.** The evidence on praise (process vs person; the Dweck line and its honest
   replication status) and on feedback that improves work without crushing motivation. I want to
   avoid "everyone's a genius" inflation that makes feedback meaningless.
5. **Making across media** (visual, music, writing, building/engineering, code) — what cross-cuts,
   and what is medium-specific, for a kid building a portfolio over years?

*Ethics:*
6. **Moral development, ages ~7 to ~21 — the honest picture.** Kohlberg and its serious critiques
   (Gilligan's care ethics; Haidt's moral-foundations / social-intuitionist view that judgement is
   often intuition-first). I do **not** want single-doctrine moralising or obedience-as-virtue; I
   want a child who can hold care, consequences, and fairness *together* and reason about them.
7. **Teaching ethical reasoning to children** without indoctrination: moral imagination ("who is
   affected"), perspective-taking, dilemma discussion (the evidence on dilemma-based moral
   education), values clarification done well. What transfers; what is preachy and useless?
8. **Environmental / ranger ethics specifically.** Age-appropriate ways into real conservation
   dilemmas (culling, intervention, human-vs-habitat) that respect a child's emotional safety while
   not lying that nature is simple. (Cross-check tone against a wildlife-for-kids safety frame.)
9. **AI-era ethics for the young.** Any credible emerging frameworks on ethics of using AI, honesty,
   attribution, and "should this be made" — for children/teens. Likely thin; flag confidence.

**Honesty constraints:**
- Tag **zeker** / **waarschijnlijk**. Be blunt where creativity-training and character-education
  programs have weak transfer evidence. Note the Dweck/growth-mindset replication debate honestly.
- **No indoctrination.** Ethics must be taught as reasoning and perspective, never as a single right
  answer to memorise. Flag anything that crosses into preaching.
- Keep emotionally heavy ethical content (death, culling, harm) age-gated and safe; flag where.

**Required output format:**
- **Rung-by-rung spirals**, ages 7→21, separately for *creativity/making* and *ethics* (table: age
  band · skill · what it actually is · realistic flag).
- A **framework menu** per area (name · one-line · evidence flag · best age · veto column blank),
  with single-doctrine moral systems flagged so I can choose to exclude them.
- **10–15 game-mechanic seeds**: maker challenges and ranger dilemmas — each with skill · loop ·
  difficulty scaling · tracking model (portfolio for making; reflection-artefact for ethics, **no
  morality score**).
- A **taste / critique mechanic deep-dive**: how to train evaluation of work (incl. AI output)
  without crushing a young maker.
- A **feedback-design section**: how to give process-focused feedback in-game that helps without
  inflation or discouragement.
- An **emotional-safety / age-gating section** for heavy ethical content.
- **Accessibility notes** (dyslexia) and **open-gaps / caveats**.
- **Sources** (cite; flag CC0/open material for clean-room rewrite).
