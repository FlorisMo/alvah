## PASTE THIS INTO DEEP RESEARCH

**Context.** I am building a long-horizon educational game for my son (currently 8, Dutch, dyslexic)
that grows with him for years, difficulty rising with skill. He plays a wildlife ranger on the
Veluwe (a Dutch heath/forest reserve). This brief is **training the senses**: sight, hearing, smell,
touch, taste — and crucially **becoming aware that you are perceiving at all** and raising the
*resolution* of that perception. It explicitly includes **interoception** (awareness of bodily
sensation), which links into a separate inner-work strand I am researching elsewhere — here I care
about interoception as a *perceptual* skill (noticing the signal finely), not as emotional
regulation. My AI-era belief: perception is irreducibly embodied — a machine cannot perceive reality
*for* you — and first-hand, high-resolution sensing is the ground beneath good observation, taste,
and presence, and an antidote to living entirely through screens and someone else's representation.

**Audience:** me (parent/designer), distilling into game mechanics and a spiral curriculum across
**ages 7 → 21**. Single learner, tablet (with real-world "go outside and notice" prompts welcome),
sometimes with a parent. The ranger theme makes this the most diegetic area I have: reading tracks,
identifying birds by call, feeling the weather turn, noticing what changed since yesterday.

**Research questions — go deep:**

1. **Perceptual learning — the real science.** Summarise the evidence that perceptual discrimination
   is *trainable* (the Gibson tradition; expertise studies — radiologists, sommeliers, birders,
   musicians, chess perception). What actually improves with training (acuity, discrimination,
   chunking, attention allocation)? How specific vs general is the transfer? Be honest about how
   *narrow* perceptual learning usually is.
2. **Developmental progression of perceptual skill, ages ~7 to ~21.** What perceptual abilities
   mature when? When can a child meaningfully train fine visual discrimination, pitch/timbre
   discrimination, smell/taste vocabulary, tactile discrimination? What is realistic vs pushed-too-
   early at each age?
3. **"Higher resolution" and *awareness* of perceiving.** The metacognitive layer the parent most
   wants: not just sharper senses but *noticing that one is sensing*, slowing down, attending on
   purpose. What practices build this — slow looking / "ways of seeing" in art education, deep
   listening and acoustic ecology (the Oliveros / soundscape tradition), mindful sensing, sensory
   vocabulary-building? Evidence vs tradition — flag honestly.
4. **Vision: looking vs seeing.** Visual attention and observation training, "drawing as seeing"
   (learning to look in order to draw), visual literacy / reading images. What transfers to everyday
   noticing? What is the honest evidence on whether "observation skill" generalises?
5. **Hearing & soundscape.** Auditory discrimination (pitch, timbre, rhythm, localisation), active/
   deep listening, identifying sounds in a natural soundscape (birdsong ID as a worked domain). What
   trains a child's ear, and how does this interact with the dyslexia profile (phonological vs
   environmental sound)?
6. **Smell, taste, touch.** The under-trained senses. Building a smell/taste vocabulary and
   discrimination (the sommelier/coffee-cupping model, age-appropriately and alcohol-free), tactile
   discrimination and texture. What is feasible and motivating for a child→teen?
7. **Interoception as perception** (the seam with my inner-work research). How is interoceptive
   *accuracy* and *awareness* measured and trained (heartbeat-detection paradigms, body-scan,
   sensation vocabulary)? Treat it here as raising resolution on bodily signal; note where it hands
   off to emotional-regulation use (which I research separately) so the two don't duplicate.
8. **Naturalist observation as the integrating practice.** Nature journaling, the "sit spot",
   tracking and sign-reading, phenology (noticing seasonal change). This is the diegetic heart of my
   game. What is the evidence/best-practice for building a young naturalist's perception, and what
   makes the habit stick?
9. **Psychophysics I can build on.** Which classic discrimination paradigms (two-alternative forced
   choice, just-noticeable-difference, adaptive staircases / method of constant stimuli) port to a
   kid-friendly tablet game and yield a clean, adaptive difficulty signal? This matters because my EF
   engines already use an adaptive staircase — I want to reuse that machinery for sensory acuity.

**Honesty constraints:**
- Tag claims **zeker** / **waarschijnlijk**. Perceptual learning is *real but narrow* — be explicit
  about how little it generalises, and flag where "training observation makes you generally more
  observant" is unproven.
- Distinguish well-evidenced perceptual training from contemplative/mindfulness traditions that are
  valuable but thinly evidenced — I want both, clearly labelled.
- No pathologising of sensory sensitivity or sensory difference; no "sensory overload = failure"
  framing. This is about enrichment, not deficit.

**Required output format:**
- **Rung-by-rung spirals**, ages 7→21, for *vision*, *hearing*, *smell/taste/touch*, *interoception*,
  and *integrated naturalist observation* (a table per strand: age band · skill · what it actually
  is · realistic / push-too-early flag).
- A **framework menu** per strand (name · one-line · evidence flag · best age · veto column blank),
  clearly separating measured perceptual-learning methods from contemplative sensing practices.
- **10–15 game-mechanic seeds**, leaning hard into ranger diegesis (ID the call, spot what changed,
  read the track, match the texture, slow-look journal) — each with skill · core loop · difficulty
  scaling · and **which tracking model** (Model A psychophysics-staircase for discrimination; Model B
  portfolio for observation/awareness).
- A **psychophysics deep-dive**: the 2–3 discrimination paradigms most worth building, with the
  adaptive-staircase math, mapped onto reusing my existing EF staircase engine.
- A **"noticing" / awareness mechanic deep-dive**: how to train the metacognitive layer (that you
  are perceiving, on purpose, at higher resolution) without it becoming a chore.
- **Accessibility notes** for a dyslexic 8-year-old (audio-first where possible; visual vs textual
  load; the auditory-discrimination ↔ phonological-processing interaction).
- An **open-gaps / caveats** section (especially the generalisation limits) and **sources** (cite;
  flag CC0/open material I could clean-room rewrite).
