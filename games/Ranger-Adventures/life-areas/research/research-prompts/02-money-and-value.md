## PASTE THIS INTO DEEP RESEARCH

**Context.** I am building a long-horizon educational game for my son (currently 8, Dutch, dyslexic)
that he can keep playing for years, difficulty growing with skill. This brief is **financial &
quantitative life skills**: budgeting, saving, delayed gratification, compounding, risk, expected
value, investing, inflation. My belief: compounding and delayed gratification are real life leverage
that AI does *not* grant you, so money sense is one of the highest-return things I can teach early. I
want it framed as **a skill that pays**, never as a virtue test or a get-rich pitch, and with **no
gambling-shaped reward loops** in the game itself.

**Audience:** me (parent/designer), distilling into game mechanics and a spiral curriculum across
**ages 7 → 21** — ending (per area) in a possible hand-off to a *real* (or real-feeling) budget and
portfolio with a reflection layer. Single learner, tablet, no classroom.

**Research questions — go deep:**

1. **Developmental progression of money understanding, ages ~7 to ~21.** When do children actually
   grasp: deferred value, opportunity cost, percentages, exponential/compounding growth, risk, and
   diversification? Cite the developmental-economics / financial-literacy evidence. Flag what is
   realistically teachable when, and the classic too-early traps.
2. **Delayed gratification — the honest version.** What does the evidence *actually* say now (the
   marshmallow-test reappraisals, the role of trust/environment)? How do I teach waiting as a
   *trainable, situational skill* rather than a fixed virtue or a character verdict? What in-game
   loops build it without being manipulative?
3. **Compounding & exponential intuition.** Best-evidenced ways to make "interest on interest" and
   exponential growth *felt* by a child (visual, manipulative, simulation). Where do adults' own
   misintuitions about exponentials come from, and how to inoculate early?
4. **Budgeting & trade-offs.** What budgeting models work for kids→teens (envelope/jar systems,
   allowance structures, "pay yourself first")? Evidence on allowance design that builds skill vs
   entitlement. How to model fixed vs variable, needs vs wants, without moralising.
5. **Risk, expected value, and probability applied to money** (building on brief 1's EV work).
   How to teach that the sure thing isn't always best, and the high-EV bet isn't always right given
   ruin risk — at an age-appropriate depth. Distinguish *teaching EV* from *normalising gambling*.
6. **Investing basics for the teen years.** Index-fund-first, time-in-market, inflation eroding
   idle cash, fees compounding against you. What is the minimal, honest, non-hype curriculum? What
   real or simulated instruments are appropriate at 13 / 16 / 18?
7. **The graduation question.** When and how do financial-education programs successfully hand off
   from simulation to *real stakes* (real allowance, real savings account, real small portfolio)?
   What evidence exists on real-money-with-guardrails vs pure simulation for retention and transfer?

**Honesty constraints:**
- Tag claims **zeker** / **waarschijnlijk**. Be explicit where financial-literacy *programs* have
  weak evidence of changing real behaviour — this field overclaims; I want the truth.
- Flag and reject any mechanic that trains gambling reflexes (variable-ratio reward, loot-box
  shapes, "double or nothing" dopamine loops). I want EV understood, not gambling normalised.
- No get-rich / hustle framing. The frame is stewardship and leverage, not winning.

**Required output format:**
- A **rung-by-rung spiral**, ages 7→21 (table: age band · concept · what it actually is · realistic /
  too-early flag), consistent with the worked example I already sketched (wants/needs → budget →
  compounding → risk/EV → investing → real portfolio).
- A **framework menu** (name · one-line · evidence flag · best age · veto column blank).
- **10–15 game-mechanic seeds** (skill · core loop · difficulty scaling · gambling-safety note ·
  which tracking model — most of these are mastery/portfolio, some are scored puzzles).
- A **compounding-mechanic deep-dive** and an **EV/risk-mechanic deep-dive** with the actual math
  and how to keep them honest and non-addictive.
- A **graduation playbook**: signals that Alvah is ready to move from simulated to real money, and
  how to add real stakes safely with a parent in the loop.
- **Accessibility notes** (dyslexia: number-heavy content, audio support, visual-first).
- **Open-gaps / caveats** and **sources** (cite; flag CC0/open curricula I could clean-room rewrite).
