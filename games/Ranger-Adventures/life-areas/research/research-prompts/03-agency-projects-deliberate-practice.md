## PASTE THIS INTO DEEP RESEARCH

**Context.** I am building a long-horizon educational game for my son (currently 8, Dutch, dyslexic)
that grows with him for years. This brief is **agency and getting things done**: turning a vague
urge into a defined project (scope, milestones, a done-state), setting goals and targets, and
actually finishing — plus the meta-skill of *deliberate practice* (getting better at getting
better). My belief: when AI makes execution cheap, deciding *what* to do and seeing it through is
the part that stays human. I personally work in self-organisation (Holacracy/Sociocracy; I use a
tool built on "tensions → proposals"), and I want the framing to lean that way — distributed
authority, sensing a tension, turning it into action — rather than top-down task management.

**Audience:** me (parent/designer), distilling into game mechanics and a spiral curriculum across
**ages 7 → 21**, with a likely hand-off to *real* project tooling as he matures. Single learner,
tablet, sometimes with a parent.

**Research questions — go deep:**

1. **Developmental progression of self-management & executive *use* (not just capacity), ages ~7
   to ~21.** When can a child realistically hold: a multi-step plan, a milestone, a deadline,
   several projects at once, a long-horizon goal? Distinguish EF *capacity* (which the game already
   trains) from EF *deployment* in real projects.
2. **Goal-setting that actually works for young people.** Evidence on goal-setting theory (Locke &
   Latham) adapted for kids, OKR-style objective/target framing at kid scale, implementation
   intentions ("if-then" plans, Gollwitzer), and process vs outcome goals. What helps a child finish
   vs what just produces anxiety or abandoned goals?
3. **Deliberate practice (Ericsson) for children.** The honest version: what is genuinely supported
   (focused, feedback-rich, edge-of-ability practice) vs the popularised "10,000 hours" myth. How do
   you teach a *child* to practise deliberately without killing intrinsic motivation? How does this
   interact with play?
4. **Project-management frameworks, kid-appropriate.** GTD (capture/clarify/organise), kanban /
   visible work-in-progress, agile/scrum-for-kids, "definition of done." Which concepts transfer to
   a 9-, 13-, 16-year-old, and how much structure is too much at each age?
5. **Self-organisation frameworks (my lean).** Holacracy/Sociocracy and the **tension → proposal**
   loop, distributed authority, roles vs souls. Is there any literature or credible practice on
   teaching *children* sensing-and-responding / distributed-authority rather than obedience-to-task?
   How would I scaffold "notice a tension, propose a change" for a kid?
6. **Motivation & autonomy.** Self-Determination Theory (autonomy/competence/relatedness — Deci &
   Ryan) as it bears on agency: how to design so the *child* owns the goal, and how extrinsic
   game rewards can crowd *out* intrinsic motivation (the over-justification effect). I want to
   avoid building a Skinner box.
7. **The graduation question.** When/how do young people best move from "practising projects in a
   sandbox" to running their own real projects with real tools (a real board, real commitments)?
   Signals of readiness; pitfalls.

**Honesty constraints:**
- Tag **zeker** / **waarschijnlijk**. Be blunt about deliberate-practice and goal-setting myths.
- Flag the **over-justification / Skinner-box risk** explicitly: where do points/badges/streaks help
  vs corrode the very agency I am trying to build?
- No corporate-productivity hustle framing. The goal is a self-directed kid, not a tiny employee.

**Required output format:**
- A **rung-by-rung spiral**, ages 7→21 (table: age band · capability · what it actually is ·
  realistic / push-too-early flag): one-step task → multi-step plan → milestone → goal+target →
  parallel projects → real self-run project board.
- A **framework menu** (name · one-line · evidence flag · best age · veto column blank), with the
  self-organisation/tension-loop options called out since that is my lean.
- **10–15 game-mechanic seeds** for diegetic "restoration projects on the reserve" (scope a project,
  hit a milestone, sense a tension and propose a fix) — each with skill · loop · difficulty scaling ·
  tracking model (mostly mastery/portfolio).
- A **deliberate-practice mechanic deep-dive**: how to make edge-of-ability, feedback-rich practice
  feel like play, not drilling.
- A **motivation-design section**: concrete rules for using (or *not* using) points/badges/streaks
  so they don't crowd out intrinsic motivation.
- A **graduation playbook** (signals + how to hand off to a real board — feeds PLAN §7).
- **Accessibility notes** (dyslexia; reading/planning load) and **open-gaps / caveats**.
- **Sources** (cite; flag CC0/open material for clean-room rewrite).
