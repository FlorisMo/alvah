# Agency: Projects, Goals & Getting Things Done — A Research Brief for a Long-Horizon Educational Game (Ages 7–21)

*Audience: the parent/designer. Honesty tags used throughout: **zeker** = well-established; **waarschijnlijk** = probable/suggestive. The plain framing the whole report serves: "decide what you want, split it into small chunks, and get it done."*

## TL;DR
- The skill the parent wants to build — turning a vague urge into a defined, finished project — is **deployment** of executive function in self-directed work, which matures far later and far more unevenly than EF *capacity*. Reliable future orientation and independent planning are not adult-like until ~16+ and keep developing into the early 20s, so the spiral must move from one-step tasks (age 7) to a self-run project board (16+) very gradually.
- The evidence backbone is solid for a few specific tools — **implementation intentions ("if-then" plans), specific-and-challenging goals, learning (process) goals over outcome goals for novel tasks, feedback-rich edge-of-ability practice, and GTD-style externalising of commitments** — and equally solid that **tangible, expected, contingent rewards reliably corrode intrinsic motivation** (Deci, Koestner & Ryan 1999: free-choice intrinsic motivation undermined at d = −0.40, −0.36, −0.28 for engagement-, completion-, and performance-contingent rewards; worse for children). Points/badges/streaks are the single biggest design risk to the very agency the parent is trying to grow.
- Build the game as a **mastery/portfolio sandbox** that fades scaffolding *contingently* (the strongest "when to hand off" evidence is the expertise-reversal effect and Zimmerman's four-level observation→emulation→self-control→self-regulation ladder); graduate the child to real tools when they show recognition-AND-production independence and self-initiated capture — typically piloting around 12–13 and running real boards by 15–16.

## Key Findings

**1. Capacity ≠ deployment (zeker).** Developmental neuroscience is clear that the *components* of executive function (working memory, inhibition, cognitive flexibility, planning) emerge early and are largely adult-like on laboratory tasks by mid-adolescence, though they keep refining into the 20s. But the parent's target skill is not lab capacity — it is *spontaneous deployment* of these abilities in messy, self-chosen, low-cue real projects. That deployment depends on prospective memory (remembering to do the intended thing without a prompt), future orientation, and self-regulation, all of which mature later than raw capacity. A child can *hold* a plan in a test long before they will *use* one unprompted on their own project.

**2. Future orientation matures late (zeker).** Steinberg et al. (2009, *Child Development*, N=935 aged 10–30): "Younger adolescents consistently demonstrate a weaker orientation to the future than do individuals aged 16 and older… Planning ahead, in contrast, continues to develop into young adulthood." Delay discounting (preferring smaller-sooner reward) peaks in adolescence and plateaus only in late adolescence. **Implication:** long-horizon goals (months) are developmentally unrealistic to *own* before mid-adolescence; the game must supply the future-orientation scaffolding (visible milestones, near-term done-states) that the child's brain cannot yet supply for itself.

**3. Prospective memory rises across childhood into adolescence (zeker/waarschijnlijk).** PM follows a broadly improving (often J-shaped) trajectory from early childhood, with adolescents approaching adult performance; it is driven by retrospective memory, executive function, attention, metamemory, and motivation. This is the cognitive substrate of "capture" — and a key reason the *system* (external capture) must do the remembering while the child is young.

**4. Goal-setting: what works vs. folklore (zeker, with flags).** Locke & Latham's goal-setting theory (specific, challenging goals beat "do your best," moderated by feedback, commitment, and task complexity) is among the most replicated findings in motivation science — but it was built on adult workplace performance and is mostly *assigned-goal* research. Two honesty flags: (a) For **novel/complex tasks**, a specific *outcome* goal can cause "tunnel vision" and worsen learning; Locke & Latham themselves recommend **learning goals** ("learn to do X") over performance goals in that case — directly relevant to a child learning to run projects. (b) **SMART goals are not Locke & Latham** and are widely cargo-culted; SMART says nothing about whether a goal is worth pursuing or appropriately challenging.

**5. Implementation intentions are the strongest single transferable tool (zeker).** Gollwitzer & Sheeran's (2006) meta-analysis (94 tests, >8,000 participants) found if-then plans ("If situation Y, then I will do Z") produce a medium-to-large effect on goal attainment (d≈0.65) over goal intentions alone. The 2024 update (Sheeran, Listrom & Gollwitzer, *European Review of Social Psychology* 36(1):162–194, **642 tests**) confirms effectiveness "for cognitive, affective, and behavioural outcomes (.27 ≤ d ≤ .66)," with larger effects when plans use a genuine if-then format, the person is motivated, and the plan is rehearsed at least once. Critically for this project, **the 31 effect sizes from children under 12 yielded an average d ≈ 0.43 (95% CI [0.27, 0.59])** — this tool genuinely works for young children, not just adults. Highly game-able and age-robust.

**6. Deliberate practice — the honest version (zeker).** Ericsson's deliberate practice (focused, feedback-rich, edge-of-ability practice with immediate correction, ideally coached) is genuinely supported as *a* driver of skill. But the popular "10,000 hours" rule is a Gladwell popularisation Ericsson himself disputed (10,000 was an *average* for violinists by age 20, who "were nowhere near masters"). Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald (2014, *Psychological Science*): "deliberate practice explained 26% of the variance in performance for games, 21% for music, 18% for sports, 4% for education, and less than 1% for professions." Important, but far from the whole story. **Design consequence:** teach the *mechanics* of deliberate practice (tight feedback loops, working just past current ability) without promising that mastery is merely a function of hours, and without crushing play.

**7. Self-Determination Theory is the master key (zeker).** Deci & Ryan: autonomy, competence, and relatedness drive higher-quality, more durable motivation. In education, autonomy-supportive environments produce better self-regulation, performance, and wellbeing. The whole point of the parent's project is *autonomous* motivation — the child owning the goal — so SDT is the design constitution, and anything that makes the child feel controlled (including rewards) is suspect.

**8. The over-justification / Skinner-box risk is real and worse for children (zeker).** Lepper, Greene & Nisbett's (1973) classic marker study: preschoolers who *expected* a reward for drawing later drew much less in free play than those who got an unexpected reward or none. Deci, Koestner & Ryan's (1999, *Psychological Bulletin* 125(6):627–668) meta-analysis of 128 studies: "engagement-contingent, completion-contingent, and performance-contingent rewards significantly undermined free-choice intrinsic motivation (d = −0.40, −0.36, and −0.28, respectively)," while positive *feedback* enhanced free-choice behaviour (d = 0.33). They explicitly note **tangible rewards are more detrimental for children than for college students.** This is the central tension of the entire build: the easiest engagement mechanics (points, badges, streaks) are precisely the ones most likely to corrode the agency being cultivated.

**9. Frameworks transfer partially (waarschijnlijk).** GTD's *capture* and *next-action* logic, personal kanban's *visualise + limit work-in-progress*, and "definition of done" are simple enough to adapt for a 9-year-old in stripped form; the full GTD horizons/weekly-review apparatus and scrum team roles suit 13+; full self-managed scrum/eduScrum suits mid-adolescence. eduScrum has practitioner reports of engagement gains and modest grade improvements but **little controlled evidence** — treat as promising, not proven.

**10. Graduation = contingent fading (zeker on mechanism).** The best-evidenced "when to hand off" findings are the **expertise-reversal effect** (Kalyuga, Ayres, Chandler & Sweller 2003; Kalyuga 2007: guidance that helps novices becomes redundant or actively harmful for more advanced learners — so scaffolding *must* fade) and **Zimmerman's four-level model** (observation → emulation → self-control → self-regulation; Schunk & Zimmerman 1997). Readiness is signalled by adaptive, independent strategy use across novel conditions — not smooth in-the-moment performance, which can be a "fluency illusion" (Bjork & Bjork 2011).

## Details

### Developmental progression — capacity vs. deployment

The literature distinguishes EF *components* maturing at different rates: cognitive flexibility becomes more complex around 7–9 and roughly adult-like by ~15–17 (with some evidence of continued gains into the 20s); working memory and planning keep improving across adolescence into young adulthood. But the parent's interest is **deployment in self-directed projects**, gated by three later-maturing systems:

- **Prospective memory** (remembering intentions without a cue): improving steadily through childhood, near-adult in adolescence. This is why young children need the *system* to hold commitments.
- **Future orientation / time horizon**: weak before ~16, with planning-ahead continuing to develop into the 20s (Steinberg 2009). Long-horizon goals are not developmentally ownable early.
- **Self-regulation** (Zimmerman's forethought → performance → self-reflection cycle): teachable and improvable, but the *self-directed* version arrives in stages.

A blunt synthesis: a 9-year-old can genuinely execute a 2–3 step plan **with external structure and near-term payoff**; a 13-year-old can hold a multi-week milestone **with reminders and a visible board**; a 16-year-old can begin to own a multi-month goal and juggle parallel projects **but still benefits from review scaffolding**. Pushing long-horizon, multi-project self-management before mid-adolescence mostly produces abandoned goals and anxiety, not agency.

### Goal-setting, implementation intentions, and finishing

What genuinely helps a child *finish*: (a) **specific, proximal, achievable-this-week sub-goals** (proximal goals beat distal ones for young learners and for self-efficacy); (b) **learning/process goals** for anything new ("figure out how to…", "do three reps of…") rather than outcome goals that invite tunnel-vision and anxiety; (c) **implementation intentions** that pre-decide the when/where/how; (d) **feedback** (a goal without feedback is inert in Locke & Latham); (e) **commitment/ownership** (self-set or co-set goals raise commitment). What produces anxiety or abandonment: vague big goals, distant outcome goals with no proximal steps, assigned goals the child doesn't own, and SMART-template box-ticking that never asks "is this the right goal?"

### Deliberate practice without killing play

Deliberate practice and play are often framed as opposites (play = immediate gratification; deliberate practice = delayed). The reconciliation supported by the youth-sport literature: children in free **play** accumulate more time-on-task and higher autonomy/enjoyment than in many structured drills (where 25–54% of "training" time is often spent waiting), and early diversified play often beats early specialisation. The honest design move is to embed deliberate-practice *features* — clear target just beyond current ability, immediate specific feedback, repetition with correction — inside play-shaped loops, while keeping the child's sense of choice intact. Avoid the drill-sergeant framing entirely.

### Project-management frameworks, by age

- **GTD (David Allen): capture → clarify → organise → reflect → engage.** Transferable kernels: *get it out of your head* (capture), the *two-minute rule*, "if it takes >1 step it's a project; define the next action," and the *weekly review*. Allen explicitly works bottom-up (control daily inputs before big-picture goals) — developmentally apt for children. GTD's six "Horizons of Focus" are for adults.
- **Scope→done→milestones (the parent's spine):** one-sentence story ("As a [role] I want [goal] so that [benefit]"), Occam's-razor minimum scope, a binary checkable definition of done, 2–5 milestones with milestone-1 achievable in week one, titles describing the *end state*. This is excellent and evidence-aligned (proximal goals + clear done-state + commitment).
- **Personal kanban (Benson & Barry):** just two rules — *visualise your work* and *limit work-in-progress*. The WIP limit is the secret weapon against the universal tendency to start many things and finish none. A visible "Done" column gives competence feedback without points.
- **eduScrum / scrum-for-kids:** roles, sprints, stand-ups, retrospectives, "definition of done." Engagement-positive in practitioner reports; controlled evidence thin. Best mid-adolescence and in teams; overkill for a solo 9-year-old.

### Light touch: capture as the front door to initiative

"Notice what bugs you and turn it into a defined project" is, in everyday terms, GTD's capture step married to project-based-learning's "need to know" and student agency. PBL has reasonable (if quasi-experimental, fidelity-dependent) support across ages; Reggio-style emergent curriculum and student agency are credible traditions for *child-led* initiative. Keep this as flavour, not a research focus — and avoid governance/Holacracy framing entirely, as instructed.

### Motivation design — the heart of the matter

The mechanism is the over-justification effect mediated by SDT's cognitive evaluation theory: rewards that feel **controlling** ("do this to get that") undermine intrinsic motivation; rewards that feel **informational** ("this marks genuine mastery") can support competence. Most gamification defaults to controlling because it's easy to build ("complete lesson → XP"). The K-12 evidence is double-edged: Bai, Hew & Huang (2020, *Educational Research Review* 30:100322) found gamification produced a significant medium positive effect on learning (Hedges' g = 0.504), but warned that "rewards such as points and badges can reinforce extrinsically motivated behavior [and] may shift the focus of students to the rewards rather than the learning," and that leaderboards "may increase the sense of embarrassment for students in low positions." K-12 meta-analyses consistently find gamification boosts *extrinsic* motivation more than intrinsic. The design rule: make the reward *be* the genuine evidence of competence (a finished, visible artefact; a portfolio entry), not a detachable token.

### Accessibility — dyslexia and cognitive/planning load

For a dyslexic 8-year-old: minimise reading and planning text. Use clear sans-serif fonts (Arial/Verdana/Open Sans/Lexend; specialised faces like OpenDyslexic/Dyslexie are options but evidence for them over good sans-serifs is mixed), generous letter/line spacing (~1.5×), left-aligned (never justified) text, ≥16px body, high contrast (≥4.5:1 per WCAG), no italics for body, short lines (~60–70 chars). Offer **audio/voice capture and text-to-speech** so the child can capture ideas and read instructions without the reading bottleneck becoming the project bottleneck. Use icons + colour + voice to carry planning information, not paragraphs.

### Game-mechanic seeds — theme: restoration projects on a nature reserve

1. **Scope-the-Restoration.** *Skill:* cutting a vague urge to minimum viable scope. *Loop:* spot a degraded patch → write one-sentence story ("As a ranger I want the pond edge clear so frogs return") → pick smallest version → set binary done. *Scaling:* younger = pick from 2 pre-scoped options; older = scope from scratch & defend the cut. *Tracking:* portfolio of "scoped projects," mastery rating on *tightness of scope*.
2. **Notice-and-Name (capture).** *Skill:* sense-and-respond initiative. *Loop:* wander reserve → tap anything that "bugs you" → it drops into an inbox → later clarify into project / reference / discard. *Scaling:* younger = voice/photo capture; older = clarify a backlog under mild time pressure. *Tracking:* ratio of self-initiated captures that became finished projects (not a point total).
3. **Milestone-Marker.** *Skill:* breaking work into checkpoints. *Loop:* split a restoration into 2–5 visible stakes along a trail; reaching one reveals the next vista. *Scaling:* younger = milestones pre-placed; older = child places them and must make milestone-1 reachable "today." *Tracking:* milestone map fills in (visual portfolio).
4. **Definition-of-Done Gate.** *Skill:* binary finish lines. *Loop:* before starting, set the checkable done ("10 saplings planted AND watered"); a gate won't open until done is literally true. *Scaling:* younger = checklist supplied; older = writes own done and learns to make it checkable. *Tracking:* mastery on "was your done actually checkable?"
5. **If-Then Ranger Cards.** *Skill:* implementation intentions. *Loop:* attach an if-then to each project ("If it's raining, then I sort seeds indoors"); when the trigger fires in-game, the plan auto-surfaces. *Scaling:* younger = pick from a deck; older = author and rehearse. *Tracking:* % of triggered plans actually executed.
6. **WIP-Limited Toolshed.** *Skill:* limiting work-in-progress. *Loop:* the shed has only N tool-pegs; you can't start a new restoration until you finish one and free a peg. *Scaling:* N stays small to teach focus. *Tracking:* throughput (finished ÷ started), visible "Done meadow."
7. **Edge-of-Ability Trail (deliberate practice).** *Skill:* practising just past current ability with feedback. *Loop:* a skill (e.g., dry-stone wall building) offers a rung one step harder than last success, with instant visual feedback on each placement. *Scaling:* difficulty auto-tunes to keep ~70–85% success. *Tracking:* mastery ladder; portfolio of "personal-best" attempts, not scores.
8. **Weekly Reserve Walk (reflect).** *Skill:* review/self-regulation. *Loop:* a recurring guided walk past all live projects → mark progress, re-scope, retire stale ones. *Scaling:* younger = 3-question walk; older = full review of parallel projects. *Tracking:* reflection-journal entries (portfolio).
9. **Before/After Lens (portfolio).** *Skill:* seeing finished output. *Loop:* every finished restoration is photographed before/after into a reserve almanac. *Scaling:* older learners annotate what they'd do differently. *Tracking:* the almanac *is* the reward — competence made visible, no points.
10. **The Itch Log.** *Skill:* turning tension into action. *Loop:* a private log of "things that bug me about the reserve"; periodically the game asks "want to turn one into a project?" *Scaling:* older = log feeds a backlog they prioritise. *Tracking:* conversion rate itch→project→done.
11. **Help-Fade Mentor.** *Skill:* graduating off scaffolding. *Loop:* an NPC ranger gives less help each time you succeed at a task type (contingent fading); eventually "you don't need me here." *Scaling:* fade rate tied to demonstrated independence. *Tracking:* "flying solo" markers that *retire* the mentor (informational, not collectible).
12. **Re-scope Rescue.** *Skill:* cutting scope when stuck. *Loop:* a stalled project triggers a prompt: "shrink the done-state to something finishable this week?" *Scaling:* younger = suggested smaller scope; older = must propose it. *Tracking:* recoveries logged in portfolio.
13. **Two-Minute Path.** *Skill:* GTD two-minute rule. *Loop:* tiny tasks (<2 min) are flagged "just do it now"; doing them clears the trail visibly. *Scaling:* child learns to *judge* what's a two-minute job. *Tracking:* clutter cleared (visual).
14. **Season-Long Stewardship (long horizon).** *Skill:* multi-month goal ownership (older only). *Loop:* adopt a corner of the reserve for a whole in-game season with self-set milestones. *Scaling:* unlocks at ~15–16 equivalent maturity. *Tracking:* season almanac + self-evaluation.
15. **Predict-Then-Check (calibration).** *Skill:* honest self-assessment / metacognition. *Loop:* before a task, the child predicts "will this be done by Friday?"; afterward the game shows prediction vs. reality, gently. *Scaling:* younger = simple yes/no; older = estimate effort and compare. *Tracking:* calibration accuracy over time (counters over-confidence, which is high until ~7–8 and keeps calibrating through late childhood).

### Deliberate-practice mechanic deep-dive

Make edge-of-ability practice feel like play, not drilling, with five moves: (1) **Auto-tuned difficulty** keeping success ~70–85% so the child is always *almost* able — the flow zone; never a wall, never trivial. (2) **Immediate, specific, task-level feedback** (Hattie's feedback work points to task/process/self-regulation levels) — show *what* to adjust on the next attempt, not a grade. (3) **Repetition with variation** (interleaving/varied practice — a "desirable difficulty," Bjork & Bjork 2011) so it feels like exploring, not rote. (4) **Player-chosen targets** — the child picks which sub-skill to push, preserving autonomy (SDT). (5) **Visible personal-best trajectory** (mastery ladder/portfolio) instead of points, so the reward is competence made visible. Critically, **guard against the fluency illusion**: smooth in-session performance is not learning; use spacing and delayed re-tests so the child (and parent) judge readiness on durable performance, not in-the-moment ease.

### Motivation-design rules (repo-wide important)

**Default to NO points/badges/streaks.** Use them only where they genuinely *inform competence* and never where they could *control* behaviour on already-interesting tasks. Concrete rules:

1. **Reward the artefact, not the activity.** The finished, visible restoration (before/after almanac) *is* the reward. Don't bolt detachable tokens onto intrinsically interesting work — that's exactly the Lepper marker-study trap.
2. **No expected, tangible, contingent rewards for creative/agency tasks** — the most-undermining category (d = −0.28 to −0.40; worse for children). Note the flip side: positive *informational feedback* enhanced free-choice behaviour (d = 0.33) in the same meta-analysis. Acknowledgement after the fact is least harmful when it informs competence rather than dangles a prize.
3. **Make any "badge" informational, not collectible.** A badge should certify genuine mastery ("you can scope a project solo") and ideally *retire a scaffold* rather than accumulate. Avoid leaderboards entirely for a single learner; they add nothing and risk demotivation (Bai et al.: leaderboards can embarrass low-ranked learners).
4. **No streaks that punish.** Streaks manufacture compulsion (the Skinner-box risk) and turn a missed day into guilt — corrosive to autonomy. If you want continuity, show a *gentle progress map*, not a breakable chain.
5. **Protect autonomy at every choice point.** Let the child choose projects, scope, and which skills to push (SDT autonomy). Framing should be invitational ("want to…?"), never controlling ("you must…").
6. **Competence feedback must be honest.** Children are systematically over-confident until ~7–8 and calibration keeps improving through late childhood; feedback should gently improve calibration (predict-then-check), not inflate it.
7. **Relatedness via the parent, not the app.** The "sometimes a parent is present" feature is an asset: shared review walks satisfy relatedness without the app manufacturing social pressure.

### Graduation playbook

**Principle:** scaffolding must *fade contingently* — the expertise-reversal effect (Kalyuga/Sweller) shows support that helps a novice becomes redundant or harmful once they're competent; but premature release collapses into an unguided assignment. So fade on *evidence*, not on a schedule.

**Readiness signals (hand off more when these appear):**
- **Recognition AND production independence** (Wood, Bruner & Ross 1976): the child can both recognise a good plan/done-state *and* produce one unprompted. (Their pyramid-building study found children recognise correct solutions before they can produce them — close that gap before handing off.)
- **Self-initiated capture**: they notice-and-name projects without being told to.
- **Adaptive strategy use across novel conditions** (Zimmerman level 4: self-regulation, not mere emulation) — they adjust the approach when a new project differs, rather than replicating a template.
- **Longer independent stretches** between help requests (Wood/Bruner/Ross's operations-per-intervention rising with maturity).
- **Durable performance on delayed/transfer checks**, not just smooth in-session performance (Bjork's fluency caution).

**Staged handoff:**
1. **Sandbox with mentor (≈7–12):** in-game board, contingent NPC help; the system holds capture and reminders.
2. **Hybrid pilot (≈12–14):** child runs a *real* small project (a real toolshed corner, a real hobby build) using a simplified real board (paper kanban or a kid-simple app), with parent as reviewer, not director.
3. **Real tools, real commitments (≈15–17):** child adopts a genuine personal kanban / lightweight GTD with real stakes and a weekly review; parent fades to occasional check-in.

**Pitfalls:** Handing off **too early** → overwhelm, abandoned projects, dependence-collapse (future orientation isn't there yet pre-16). Handing off **too late** → dependency/learned-helplessness risk and the expertise-reversal drag of pointless scaffolding; the child stops feeling ownership. Watch for the child *asking* for more autonomy — a strong, honest signal. (Note: "productive failure," Sinha & Kapur 2021, *Review of Educational Research*, 53 studies, g≈0.36 for concept learning, supports letting the child *struggle first* before offering help — a useful design instinct for the older bands.)

### Accessibility notes (dyslexia + planning load)
- **Reading:** clear sans-serif, ≥16px, ~1.5× line spacing, left-aligned, high contrast (≥4.5:1), no justified text, no italics for body, short lines.
- **Modality:** voice capture + text-to-speech everywhere; icons/colour/audio carry planning info, not paragraphs.
- **Planning load:** one decision at a time; pre-fill templates; binary done-states (checkbox, not essay); WIP limit doubles as a cognitive-load limit.
- **Avoid:** time-pressured reading, walls of instructions, and reward systems that add a second thing to track.

## Recommendations (staged & concrete)

**Stage 0 — design constitution (now):** Adopt SDT as the non-negotiable frame and the scope→done→milestones method plus GTD-capture as the spine. Decide *up front* to default to mastery/portfolio tracking and to ban controlling rewards. *Benchmark to change course:* if play-testing shows the child chasing tokens or asking "what do I get," strip the tokens.

**Stage 1 — ages 7–10 build:** One-step capture → tiny multi-step projects with binary done-states and near-term payoff; the system holds steps and reminders. If-then cards from a deck. WIP-limited toolshed. *Benchmark:* child finishes >half of self-started tiny projects and initiates captures unprompted → advance.

**Stage 2 — ages 10–13:** Introduce milestones, the weekly Reserve Walk (review), learning goals, and authoring (not just picking) if-then plans and done-states. Begin contingent mentor-fade. *Benchmark:* child can scope from scratch, set a checkable done, and recover a stalled project by re-scoping → pilot a real small project.

**Stage 3 — ages 13–16:** Parallel projects under a WIP limit; hybrid real-world pilot with a simple real board; parent as reviewer. *Benchmark:* adaptive strategy across novel projects + self-initiated capture + asking for more autonomy → hand off real tools.

**Stage 4 — ages 16–21:** Real personal kanban/GTD, real commitments, weekly review owned by the young person; the game retires into a coaching/reflection role. *Benchmark:* durable independent running of a multi-month goal.

**Throughout:** measure the *right* thing — finished artefacts, conversion of captures→done, and calibration accuracy — not points, time-on-app, or streaks.

## Open gaps / caveats
- **eduScrum and PBL evidence is weak/quasi-experimental** — engagement claims are largely practitioner-reported; don't over-trust grade-gain figures.
- **Almost no research targets a *solo* child on a tablet building self-directed projects over 14 years** — most EF, SRL, and goal research is classroom, lab, or workplace; transfer to this exact design is inference, not direct evidence (waarschijnlijk).
- **Goal-setting theory is adult/workplace-origin and assigned-goal-heavy** — the child-ownership adaptation is theoretically sound but less directly evidenced.
- **Specialised dyslexia fonts** (OpenDyslexic/Dyslexie) lack strong evidence of superiority over good plain sans-serifs — don't rely on them as a silver bullet.
- **Marshmallow-test framing is unreliable** — Watts, Duncan & Quan (2018, *Psychological Science* 29(7), NICHD sample N=918): an extra minute waited at age 4 predicted only ~0.1 SD gain in age-15 achievement, "only half the size of those reported in the original studies and… reduced by two thirds in the presence of controls for family background, early cognitive ability, and the home environment." Do *not* build the game on "training delay of gratification" as a predictor of success.
- **Readiness checklists for handoff are heuristics, not validated thresholds** — the underlying mechanisms (expertise reversal, Zimmerman levels) are well-evidenced, but exact age boundaries are not.
- **"Over-scaffolding → learned helplessness"** is mechanistically plausible but mostly practitioner-asserted, not proven in controlled studies; the *expertise-reversal* harm of un-faded guidance, by contrast, is well-evidenced experimentally.
