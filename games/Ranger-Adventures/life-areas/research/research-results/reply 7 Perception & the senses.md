# Training the Senses & Perception — Research Dossier for the Veluwe Ranger Game
### Life area: Perception & the senses (sight · hearing · smell · taste · touch · interoception-as-perception · integrated naturalist observation). Single learner: dyslexic Dutch boy, 8 → game spirals 7–21. Tablet + "go outside" prompts.

---

## TL;DR
- **Perceptual discrimination is genuinely trainable (zeker) — but the gains are NARROW and stubbornly domain-, location-, and stimulus-specific.** The intuitive, marketable claim that "training observation makes you a generally more observant person" is **UNPROVEN (and probably false as stated)**. Design the game to deliver many *specific* wins (this bird, this track, this texture) and treat "general noticing" as a *value and a habit*, not a measurable trained skill.
- **Build TWO scoring engines.** *Model A (psychophysics staircase)* for true discrimination/acuity — and you can directly reuse your existing executive-function adaptive-staircase machinery (a 3-down/1-up rule targeting 79.4% correct is the cleanest fit). *Model B (portfolio)* for awareness, slow-looking, sit-spots and journaling — these are contemplative/observational practices that are **valuable but thinly evidenced** and must *never* be reduced to a threshold or a score, or you kill the thing you're trying to grow.
- **The dyslexia-critical point:** rhythm and musical-temporal training **plausibly (waarschijnlijk)** transfers to phonological awareness and reading; *environmental-sound and birdsong discrimination is a different, largely separate skill* with no good evidence it touches phonology. So train soundscape/birdsong for **joy, mastery and diegetic richness — not as reading therapy** — and lean audio-first throughout to play to his strengths and reduce text load.

## Key Findings

**1. Perceptual learning is real but narrow (zeker).** The Eleanor & James Gibson tradition frames perceptual learning as *differentiation* — "differentiating previously vague impressions," discovering the higher-order invariants that matter and filtering out what doesn't. A century of work confirms trained improvement in acuity, discrimination thresholds, attentional allocation, and "chunking." But the hallmark finding is **specificity**: in the classic demonstration (Karni & Sagi, 1991, *PNAS* 88:4966–4970, "Where practice makes perfect in texture discrimination: evidence for primary visual cortex plasticity"), texture-discrimination gains were specific to the *trained retinal location and orientation* and did not transfer to untrained ones. Auditory time-interval learning at 100 ms doesn't transfer to 50 or 200 ms. Infants who master sloped surfaces by crawling re-learn the whole problem from scratch when they start walking (Adolph). Transfer *can* be coaxed (e.g., the "double-training" work of Xiao/Zhang showing reweighting at decision stages), but the default assumption for a game designer must be: **a skill trained on stimulus X improves stimulus X and little else.**

**2. Expertise = perception + memory chunks, not better eyes (zeker).** Radiologists detect abnormalities in chest X-rays flashed for ~200 ms (Kundel & Nodine, 1975); chess masters reproduce real board positions after a few seconds but are no better than novices on *random* positions (Chase & Simon, 1973). Chase & Simon estimated a grandmaster holds ~50,000 chess "chunks." The lesson: experts don't have superhuman sensory hardware — they have a huge library of meaningful patterns and know where to look. **This is buildable in a game** (curated pattern libraries, spaced exposure) and it is the honest model for "becoming a ranger who reads the forest."

**3. Sensory systems mature on different clocks across 7→21 (zeker, with caveats).** Basic visual acuity and colour vision are essentially adult-like by school entry, but *perceptual decision-making, frequency discrimination, and auditory processing keep maturing through adolescence* — and much of the measured "immaturity" in children is **attention, memory and motivation, not raw sensory limits** (Moore et al.; Banai & Yifat, "Perceptual Anchoring in Preschool Children"). Pitch/voice discrimination (just-noticeable-difference) keeps falling into the teens and is *not* adult-like even at 11 (Heller Murray et al.; Nagels et al.). Tactile spatial acuity has a hard physical ceiling set by finger size and receptor spacing (Wong, Peters & Goldreich, 2013). **Design implication:** difficulty must scale with the child, but early bands should reward *engagement and breadth*; fine-threshold chasing belongs in the teen bands.

**4. Contemplative "ways of seeing/listening" are valuable but thinly evidenced (waarschijnlijk helpful; not proven).** Berger's *Ways of Seeing*, Project Zero's Artful Thinking / Visible Thinking routines (See–Think–Wonder; Looking: Ten Times Two), Oliveros's Deep Listening, Schafer's soundscape/acoustic-ecology work, John Muir Laws's nature journaling, Jon Young's sit-spot — these are rich, time-tested *traditions*. Project Zero has real classroom uptake and some program evaluation, but rigorous causal evidence that these routines raise general perceptual ability is weak; they are best understood as **dispositional and cultural scaffolds** ("patterns of thinking" woven into a routine) rather than acuity training. **Label them as such in the build.**

**5. Olfactory and taste training have a genuine evidence base; touch is trainable to a physical limit (zeker for the mechanism).** "Smell training" (repeated exposure to a small odorant set over ≥12 weeks) has level-1A evidence for *restoring/improving* olfactory function and produces measurable brain change (multiple meta-analyses). The Sapere method (Jacques Puisais, France, 1974; "to taste, to know, to feel, to be brave") builds taste/flavour *vocabulary and willingness* in children and has RCT-grade support for widening vegetable/fruit acceptance — perfect for an **entirely alcohol-free "ranger's nose/palate" strand**. Tactile two-point discrimination improves with training but only down to a finger-size-limited floor; pianists who practised from childhood have measurably finer tactile acuity than non-musicians.

**6. Interoception splits cleanly into perception vs regulation (zeker on the taxonomy).** Garfinkel et al. (2015, *Biological Psychology* 104:65–74) validated three dissociable dimensions: **interoceptive accuracy** ("the process of accurately detecting and tracking internal bodily sensations" — objective task performance), **interoceptive sensibility** (self-reported tendency to attend inward), and **interoceptive awareness** (metacognition: knowing when your perception is correct). This dossier owns *accuracy + attention*. The moment the child is asked to *label a feeling or change a state*, you've crossed into emotion-regulation (the separately-researched strand). Schaan et al. (2019) provide the evidence the two are separable in children: interoceptive accuracy "showed no connection to emotion recognition, with only a marginal link to emotion regulation."

## Details

### 1 · Perceptual learning — the real science (and its limits)
- **What improves:** discrimination thresholds (JNDs), speed, attentional weighting toward diagnostic features, and chunking. **What rarely improves:** transfer to untrained stimuli, locations, modalities, or "observation in general."
- **The mechanism debate** (useful for honest copy): early views said training sharpens low-level sensory neurons (e.g., V1 orientation tuning); newer evidence favours *reweighting* — higher decision areas learn to read existing sensory signals better. Either way, the behavioural signature is specificity (Karni & Sagi 1991).
- **Honest flag for the parent:** any in-game line implying "you'll notice more everywhere" should be reframed as "you'll notice more *of the things you've practised*, and you'll build the *habit* of looking." The generalisation claim is where pop-neuro overreaches.

### 2 · Developmental progression 7→21
- **Vision:** acuity/colour adult-like at ~6–7; *visual decision-making, search strategy, and "reading" complex scenes* keep improving with knowledge through the teens.
- **Hearing:** pitch/frequency discrimination improves through childhood; pure-tone and harmonic discrimination roughly adult-like by ~10–11 in some studies, but voice-pitch JNDs and complex pitch keep maturing into adolescence. Auditory cortex (mismatch-negativity, theta phase-locking) continues developing through adolescence (Bishop et al.). Much early-childhood "deficit" is attentional.
- **Smell/taste:** odour *discrimination* is present early; the trainable layer is **vocabulary and reference flavours** (Sapere is designed for 3–5+ and scales up).
- **Touch:** trainable but ceilinged by anatomy; fine tactile games suit older bands.
- **Realistic-vs-pushed-too-early rule:** before ~10, prioritise breadth, naming, play, and "many easy wins." Fine-threshold staircases that chase a JND are motivating and meaningful mainly from ~11–12 upward.

### 3 · "Higher resolution" & awareness of perceiving — the metacognitive layer
- The target here is **noticing that one is sensing, on purpose** — slowing down, attending deliberately, building descriptive language.
- **Best practices (tradition, not proof):** Project Zero routines (See–Think–Wonder; *Looking: Ten Times Two* — look ≥30 s, list 10 words, look again, list 10 more); John Muir Laws's mantra **"I notice… I wonder… It reminds me of…"**; Oliveros's Deep Listening (focused vs global attention); Schafer's soundwalks. These build *sensory vocabulary* and a *stance*, which plausibly support memory and engagement (Laws: "the process of attention is what makes you fall in love with the world… the notes are data").
- **Design rule:** measure *participation and richness* (did you produce observations? did your vocabulary grow?), never *correctness*.

### 4 · Vision — looking vs seeing
- **"Drawing as seeing":** Betty Edwards's *Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain* — assess critically. The **left-brain/right-brain framing is scientifically debunked** and should not appear in game copy. BUT her five concrete *perceptual* skills (seeing edges/contours, negative space, relationships/proportion, light & shadow, the gestalt) are sound and widely effective at teaching people to draw what's actually there rather than a stored symbol — a vivid demonstration of perception over preconception. Use the *exercises*, drop the *neuro-myth*.
- **Transfer honesty:** drawing makes you better at *seeing-for-drawing*; there's no solid evidence it makes you globally more observant. Frame "draw it to really see it" as a *technique for this observation*, not a brain upgrade.

### 5 · Hearing & soundscape — and the dyslexia interaction
- **Birdsong ID is an ideal worked domain:** experts ID up to ~90% of local species by ear vs ~40% for beginners; it's learnable with structured listening, spectrograms (Cornell/Macaulay, Xeno-canto), and "one song per week" pacing (RSPB). Cornell's *Bird Song Basics* is explicitly screen-reader-accessible and learnable by ear — a strong accessibility model.
- **The dyslexia question, handled carefully:** Developmental dyslexia involves **phonological-processing** difficulty, often linked to *temporal/rhythm* processing (Goswami's temporal-sampling framework; rise-time discrimination predicts later literacy). **Music/rhythm training plausibly transfers to phonology:** in Flaugnacco et al. (2015, *PLoS ONE* 10(9):e0138715; RCT NCT02316873), a music group (N=24) outperformed painting controls (N=22) on phonological awareness and reading, and crucially "the best predictor of phonological awareness was not working memory nor auditory attention but **rhythmic reproduction**." **However**, identifying a *blackbird vs a robin* is environmental-timbre/pattern discrimination, which is **not** the same as phoneme discrimination and has **no evidence base for helping reading.** Net guidance: (a) include **rhythm/beat games** because they're fun *and* may have phonological spillover; (b) include **birdsong/soundscape** for mastery and diegesis, with **no claim** of reading benefit; (c) never frame any audio game as remediation — that risks pathologising and disappointment.

### 6 · Smell, taste, touch — the under-trained senses (ALCOHOL-FREE)
- **Smell training:** adopt the clinical protocol's *form* (a small fixed odorant set, smelled attentively, ≥12 weeks, rotating sets) for a "ranger's nose" — pine, heather, soil/petrichor, fungi, crushed leaf, animal scat clues. Evidence supports that *attentive repeated exposure* improves olfactory discrimination.
- **Taste/flavour (Sapere/TastEd):** isolate the senses, name reference tastes, go "beyond I like it / I don't like it," use open prompts ("What do you notice? What does it remind you of?"). Entirely food-based, alcohol-free, parent-friendly at the kitchen table; scales from naming (age 7–9) to building a structured "flavour wheel" vocabulary (teens) modelled on coffee-cupping (never wine).
- **Touch:** texture-matching and "what is this without looking" games; two-point and texture discrimination improve with practice to an anatomical floor. A neat evidence nugget: tactile acuity even shifts with *attentional state* (a Zen-retreat study found lowered two-point thresholds), reinforcing that **attention is itself a lever on perception.**

### 7 · Interoception as perception (the seam with the inner-work strand)
- **Measurement:** the canonical objective task is **Schandry (1981) heartbeat counting**; the discrimination variant is Whitehead et al. (1977). In children it's been used from **~age 8** (e.g., heartbeat-perception/counting studies; one large child study reported excellent internal consistency, Cronbach's α ≈ 0.90). For **ages 4–6**, counting is unsuitable and the **Jumping Jack Paradigm** (Schaan et al., 2019 — do jumping jacks, then pick which of four circles matches your heart rate) is the child-appropriate substitute.
- **BIG honesty caveat (zeker):** heartbeat counting's *validity is contested* — Murphy (2024) notes "30 years of evidence challenging the validity of this task," because scores can reflect prior knowledge of one's heart rate, time-estimation, and attention rather than true interoception. So in-game, treat any "heartbeat" mechanic as **playful body-attention, not a validated accuracy meter.**
- **Child self-report:** MAIA-Y is validated from **age 7** (Jones et al., 2021), though some subscales have weak reliability.
- **The hand-off (explicit, so the two strands don't duplicate):** *Perception strand owns* — noticing and raising resolution on a bodily signal (breath, heartbeat, warmth, hunger, the feeling of cold air, muscle tension), and **naming/describing** it. *Regulation strand owns* — connecting that signal to an emotion and *doing something about it* (Body → Emotion → Action, as in Kelly Mahler's curriculum). The clean boundary: **"notice and describe" = here; "interpret the feeling and change your state" = the other strand.**

### 8 · Naturalist observation — the integrating, diegetic heart
- **Nature journaling (John Muir Laws):** "I notice / I wonder / It reminds me of." Attention, curiosity, observation are framed as **trainable skills, not gifts.** Goal is *not* pretty pictures — it's "a tool to help you see, wonder, and remember." (Diegetic gift: **Laws is himself dyslexic** and credits a biology teacher with rescuing him academically through the *joy* of observation — a powerful, age-appropriate role-model frame for this specific boy.)
- **Sit-spot (Jon Young, *What the Robin Knows*; Coyote Mentoring):** return to one chosen place often, learn the "baseline" of bird behaviour, then read the *alarms* that break it. Bird language is multidirectional listening; "if you learn to listen to the silence, you'll hear more of everything else." Tradition-grade, not lab-proven, but deeply motivating and habit-forming.
- **Phenology & tracking:** noticing seasonal change and reading sign (tracks, scat, feeding marks). The Veluwe gives ready content: the Dutch "Big Five" — **fox, badger, roe deer, wild boar, red deer** — plus the returned **wolf** (resident since 2019), **pine marten**, **adder**, and a rich bird list (Avibase/Xeno-canto have Hoge Veluwe checklists with recordings).
- **What makes it stick over years:** a fixed personal place + light cadence + a growing personal record + mentoring presence (sometimes a parent) + low-stakes wonder. Build for *streaks of returning*, not scores.

## 10–15 GAME-MECHANIC SEEDS (ranger diegesis)
*Tracking model: **A** = psychophysics staircase (acuity/discrimination) · **B** = portfolio (observation/awareness, never threshold-scored).*

1. **"Name That Call"** — Skill: birdsong/timbre ID. Loop: hear a Veluwe call → choose from 2 (then 3,4) candidates. Scaling: more confusable species, shorter clips, background noise. **Model A.**
2. **"What Changed Since Yesterday"** — Skill: visual change detection. Loop: two photos of the ranger's patch → tap the difference. Scaling: subtler/more changes, shorter look. **Model A.**
3. **"Read the Track"** — Skill: sign discrimination. Loop: match a print/scat to the animal (fox vs badger vs marten). Scaling: similar species, partial/degraded sign. **Model A** (ID) + **B** (field log of real finds).
4. **"Match the Texture"** — Skill: tactile/visual texture. Loop: feel/see a sample → match to bark/moss/sand/heather. Scaling: finer texture pairs. **Model A.**
5. **"Slow-Look Journal"** — Skill: deliberate seeing + vocabulary. Loop: 30-s look → "I notice / I wonder / reminds me of" → draw+word. Scaling: longer holds, richer vocab targets. **Model B.**
6. **"The Sit-Spot"** — Skill: baseline + alarm reading. Loop: return to chosen spot, log who's about and the "mood" of the birds. Scaling: detect alarm patterns, predict the unseen predator. **Model B.**
7. **"Ranger's Nose"** — Skill: olfactory discrimination. Loop (real-world, parent-assisted): smell the week's scent set, name & rate. Scaling: more/closer odours, blind tests. **Model A** (blind ID) + **B** (scent diary).
8. **"Beyond Like/Dislike" (Kitchen Patrol)** — Skill: taste vocabulary (Sapere). Loop: taste a food, place it on sweet/sour/bitter/salt/umami + texture wheel. Scaling: blended flavours, blind reference tastes. **Model B** (→ **A** for blind discrimination ladders).
9. **"Pitch Path"** — Skill: pitch-direction JND. Loop: two tones → "higher or lower?" Scaling: shrinking interval via staircase. **Model A.**
10. **"Rhythm of the Forest"** — Skill: beat-keeping/rhythm copy (woodpecker drums, cuckoo cadence). Loop: tap back the pattern. Scaling: longer/syncopated patterns. **Model A** (and quietly phonology-friendly).
11. **"Body Weather Report"** — Skill: interoceptive noticing. Loop: after a walk/run, locate & rate body signals (breath, heartbeat, warmth). Scaling: fainter signals, finer intensity scale. **Model B** (explicitly NOT a heartbeat-accuracy score).
12. **"Soundscape Map"** — Skill: layered listening + localisation. Loop: stand still, place each sound on a compass map by direction & distance. Scaling: more layers, closer sources. **Model B** (+ **A** for localisation pairs).
13. **"Phenology Wheel"** — Skill: seasonal-change noticing. Loop: log firsts (first heather bloom, first frost, first returning migrant). Scaling: subtler indicators, multi-year comparison. **Model B.**
14. **"Spot the Hidden Animal"** — Skill: scene chunking / camouflage. Loop: find the roe deer/adder in the scene. Scaling: better camouflage, shorter exposure. **Model A.**
15. **"Spectrogram Detective"** (teen) — Skill: visual-auditory cross-coding of calls. Loop: match call to its spectrogram. Scaling: confusable species, noisy recordings. **Model A.**

## PSYCHOPHYSICS DEEP-DIVE — the 2–3 paradigms worth building, mapped onto your existing EF staircase

**The 2–3 paradigms to build:** (1) **2-alternative forced choice (2AFC)** — "which one?" — the workhorse, immune to response-bias, kid-legible. (2) **Just-noticeable-difference (JND) via an adaptive staircase** — the *engine* that finds the level the child can just barely do. (3) Optionally **same/different (2I-2AFC)** for smell/taste where "which is stronger" is awkward.

**The transformed up-down staircase (Levitt, 1971, *J. Acoust. Soc. Am.* 49:467–477 — the foundational paper, ~4000 citations):** require a *run* of correct answers to make the task harder, but only one error to make it easier. The math: if you need *n* correct-in-a-row to step down, the staircase converges where *pⁿ = 0.5*.
- **2-down/1-up → targets 70.7% correct** (√0.5).
- **3-down/1-up → targets 79.4% correct** (cube-root of 0.5).
These are the two standard "sweet spots." **Recommendation: use 3-down/1-up (79.4%)** — it sits comfortably above chance, feels achievable to a child (≈4 of 5 right), and is trivially codable.

**Implementation recipe (hobbyist-buildable, reuses your EF engine):**
1. Pick a *stimulus dimension* with a difficulty knob (tone interval in semitones; image difference in %; odorant dilution step; texture grit difference).
2. Track responses. **3 correct in a row → make harder one step; 1 wrong → make easier one step.**
3. Use a **larger step early, smaller step after the first 2 reversals** (this is exactly what your EF staircase already does — Garcia-Pérez's fixed-step-size guidance: the step-up should be ≳ half the spread of the psychometric function for good behaviour).
4. **Threshold estimate = average of the last 6–8 reversal levels.**
5. Stop after ~10–15 reversals or a trial cap (kids have short attention — cap it).

**When to upgrade to QUEST or PSI:**
- **QUEST** (Watson & Pelli, 1983, *Perception & Psychophysics* 33:113–120) — "places each trial at the current most probable Bayesian estimate of threshold." Maintain a *prior* over the threshold, update it after each trial (Bayes), and place the next trial at the posterior **mean** (King-Smith et al. showed mean beats mode). **Use QUEST when you want fewer trials per estimate and can assume a fixed psychometric slope** — attractive precisely because children fatigue. Free, game-friendly implementations exist: **jsQUEST/jsQuestPlus** (JavaScript, open source) and PsychoPy's `QuestHandler` (Python).
- **PSI / minimum-entropy** (Kontsevich & Tyler, 1999, *Vision Research* 39:2729–2737) — maintains a **2-D posterior over threshold AND slope** and places each trial where it *minimises expected entropy* (maximises info gain). Key trade-off numbers: threshold to ~2 dB precision in **<30 trials**, but slope to the same precision needs **~300 trials**. **Only use PSI/psi-marginal (Prins, 2013) if you genuinely need the slope/sensitivity, not just threshold** — for this game you almost never will. QUEST is effectively a slope-fixed special case of PSI.

**Bottom line:** reuse your EF **3-down/1-up staircase (79.4%)** for every Model-A sensory game; optionally swap in **QUEST** where trial-economy matters (younger child, harder modality). Skip PSI.

## "NOTICING" / AWARENESS MECHANIC DEEP-DIVE — training the metacognitive layer without making it a chore

The danger: the moment you *score* "are you paying attention?", you destroy the intrinsic, exploratory stance that is the whole point. Principles:
1. **Portfolio, never threshold.** Reward *production and return* — did you make observations today? did you come back to your sit-spot? — not correctness.
2. **Use the three-beat script** ("I notice / I wonder / it reminds me of") as the universal capture verb across all senses. It's low-friction, audio-friendly, and generative.
3. **Make richness visible, not ranked.** Show a growing personal "field notebook," an expanding vocabulary cloud, a phenology wheel filling up over seasons — *collection mechanics*, not leaderboards.
4. **Borrow Deep-Listening's two attentions** as a toggle, not a test: "zoom in on one sound" vs "open out to the whole soundscape." The skill is *switching on purpose*.
5. **Cadence over intensity.** Short, frequent, optional prompts ("the heather's out — go smell it") beat long mandatory sessions. Streaks of *returning* are the success metric.
6. **Parent-as-mentor mode.** Young's and Laws's traditions are mentoring traditions; a "ranger and apprentice" co-op mode (parent + child at the sit-spot) is more faithful and more durable than solo screen time.
7. **Wonder is the reward.** Surface a real mystery ("why did all the birds go quiet just now?") rather than a gold star. Curiosity, per Laws, is the trainable engine of attention.

## ACCESSIBILITY NOTES — for a dyslexic 8-year-old
- **Audio-first by default.** Lead with sound, voice, and image; treat text as optional support. Cornell's *Bird Song Basics* (screen-reader-compatible, learnable entirely by ear) is a good design north-star.
- **Manage visual vs textual load separately.** Visual *perception* games are fine and even strengths-playing; the load to minimise is *reading/decoding text*, not looking at images. Use icons, spoken instructions, and the child's own voice notes ("speak out loud what you see," per Laws) instead of written entries.
- **The auditory-discrimination ↔ phonological-processing interaction — handle with care:** include **rhythm/beat games** (genuine possible upside for phonology, per Flaugnacco's RCT where rhythmic reproduction best predicted phonological gains) but include **birdsong/soundscape purely for mastery and joy**, with no remediation framing. Do **not** present any sensory game as fixing reading — that risks both disappointment and pathologising.
- **No deficit framing, ever.** This is **enrichment, not therapy.** Never frame sensory sensitivity or difference as "overload = failure." Laws's own dyslexia-to-naturalist story is the model: difference reframed as a way of *seeing differently*, an advantage in observation.
- **Short sessions, generous wins, child-paced.** Sapere's "at their own pace" and the staircase's "≈4-of-5-right" target both protect motivation.

## OPEN GAPS / CAVEATS
- **Generalisation is the big one.** There is *no good evidence* that training perception in one domain makes a person globally more observant. Every Model-A engine buys you a *specific* skill. Sell breadth as **a habit and a value**, not a trained trait. This is the single claim most likely to be over-promised — flag it for the parent in bold.
- **Contemplative practices are under-evidenced.** Project Zero, Deep Listening, sit-spot, nature journaling are valuable *traditions* with thin causal proof. Keep them in (the user wants them) but **labelled** and **portfolio-scored**.
- **Edwards's neuro-theory is debunked** (left/right brain) even though her *exercises* work — strip the theory from any copy.
- **Heartbeat-counting validity is contested** (Murphy 2024). Use interoception mechanics as *playful body-attention*, never as a validated accuracy meter; and keep the perception/regulation boundary sharp ("notice & describe" here; "interpret & change state" in the other strand).
- **Developmental "deficits" are often attention/memory, not sensory limits** — so poor performance in young bands ≠ a sensory problem; design for it, don't diagnose it.
- **Child-specific evidence is thinner than adult evidence** for several methods (smell training, fine tactile discrimination, interoception measurement); the protocols are adapted from adult/clinical work and should be treated as *reasonable extrapolations*, not proven child interventions.
