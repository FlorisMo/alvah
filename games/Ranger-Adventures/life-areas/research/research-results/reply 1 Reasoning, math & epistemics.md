# Reasoning, Math & Epistemics for a Long-Horizon Solo Learning Game (Ages 7→21): An Evidence Brief

*Audience: the parent/designer. Honesty tags: **zeker** = solid evidence; **waarschijnlijk** = plausible / weaker evidence. Where evidence is essentially absent (notably AI-output evaluation), it is flagged explicitly.*

## TL;DR
- **The durable, well-evidenced moves are: build number sense before procedure (CPA), train calibration through repeated confidence-rating-with-feedback, and teach verification habits (lateral reading) — but treat "logic/coding/critical-thinking transfers to general reasoning" as largely UNPROVEN.** Apply the executive-function transfer lesson everywhere: near-transfer is real (g⁺ = 0.44), far-transfer is usually not (g⁺ = 0.11, non-significant) — Kassai, Futo, Demetrovics & Takacs (2019), *Psychological Bulletin*.
- **The single most important AI-era skill — teaching kids to spot confident AI hallucination — has essentially NO direct efficacy evidence.** The best you can do is adapt the empirically-supported *lateral reading* lineage (validated on web sources) plus credible-but-untested practitioner frameworks (Caulfield's SIFT, UNESCO 2024, Common Sense Media). Build it, but label it experimental.
- **Calibration IS trainable** (strong in adults; plausible and developmentally-supported in children), and proper scoring rules (Brier) can be made kid-friendly. This is your highest-leverage, best-evidenced epistemics mechanic.

## Key Findings

1. **Developmental rungs are real but the ages are softer than Piaget claimed.** Conditional "if-then" reasoning appears far earlier than the textbook 4–5: Ling, Wong & Diamond (2021, *Frontiers in Psychology* 11:571891) showed *"children barely 3 years old can do conditional reasoning"* once color was made *"a property of the shapes… rather than of the background."* Counterfactual reasoning matures roughly 4–6 for simple cases but "mature" overdetermined-cause reasoning is ~6–12; hypothetico-deductive/formal abstract argument is genuinely an adolescent skill (~12+). The honest takeaway: **competence is task- and presentation-dependent, not stage-locked.**
2. **Number sense before procedure is well-supported as design philosophy; the specific Boaler "timed tests cause math anxiety / brain science" claims are contested and partly mis-cited.** Meanwhile cognitive science strongly supports *automaticity* of math facts via spaced retrieval. These reconcile: build fluency humanely (low-stakes, self-competitive, spaced) — not via high-stakes timed tests.
3. **Computational thinking and critical-thinking instruction show strong near-transfer, weak/unproven far-transfer.** Argument mapping (van Gelder) has the best evidence among critical-thinking methods, but mostly in adults/undergraduates and partly without clean controls.
4. **Calibration training works in adults (Good Judgment Project, Hubbard) and children's metacognitive monitoring improves with age** — younger/weaker learners are systematically overconfident. Confidence-rating with honest feedback is the mechanic with the cleanest evidence chain for "knowing how you know."
5. **Probabilistic reasoning is buildable early IF you use the right representation: natural frequencies (counts, not %), and physical chance devices.** Zhu & Gigerenzer (2006, *Cognition* 98:287–308): with probabilities children's Bayesian accuracy *"was zero"*; with natural frequencies it rose across grades to *"19%, 39%, and 53%"* (4th→6th grade), and *"sixth graders' performance with natural frequencies matched the performance of adults with probabilities."*

## Strand 1 — REASONING/LOGIC: rung-by-rung spiral (ages 7→21)

| Age band | Concept | What it actually is | Realistic vs push-too-early |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7–8 | Sorting, sequencing, classifying; simple if-then | Group by attribute; ordered series; "if X then Y" rules | **Realistic** — conditional rules emerge by age 3 with concrete stimuli |
| 7–9 | Transitive inference | "A>B, B>C, so A>C" with concrete/visual support | **Realistic** with visual support; abstract verbal form is harder |
| 8–10 | Cause vs correlation (intuitive) | "Did X make Y happen, or did they just happen together?" | **Realistic as intuition**; formal control-of-variables is later |
| 9–11 | Simple proof / "why must this be true" | Necessity vs mere observation; counterexamples | **Borderline** — concrete cases yes; general/abstract necessity pushed too early before ~11 |
| 10–12 | Counterfactual reasoning (constrained) | "If B hadn't happened, holding everything else fixed…" | **Realistic by 9–12** for overdetermined cases; earlier "counterfactuals" are often basic conditional reasoning |
| 11–13 | Control-of-variables / systematic hypothesis testing | Vary one thing at a time (Piaget pendulum) | **Realistic from ~11–12**; teachable somewhat earlier with scaffolding |
| 12–15 | Conditional logic formalised; validity vs truth | An argument can be valid but false; logical form | **Realistic adolescent skill** |
| 14–18 | Probabilistic/Bayesian-flavoured reasoning | Updating belief by degree on evidence | **Realistic** if built on frequency intuitions laid earlier |
| 16–21 | Formal/abstract argument; meta-logic | Reasoning about reasoning; proof systems; quantifiers | **Realistic late-adolescent/adult** skill |

**Push-too-early red flags:** general/abstract "proof of necessity," fully verbal transitive/conditional logic divorced from concrete referents, and treating the formal-operational pendulum task as achievable in early primary.

## Strand 3 — EPISTEMICS (knowing how you know): rung-by-rung spiral

| Age band | Concept | What it actually is | Realistic vs push-too-early |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7–8 | "How sure am I?" (3-point) | Sure / not sure / guessing — coarse confidence | **Realistic**; keep it coarse and concrete |
| 8–10 | Confidence 1–5 + outcome feedback | Rate, then see if right; notice over/under-confidence | **Realistic**; expect strong overconfidence baseline |
| 8–10 | "Where did I learn this?" source tagging | I saw it / someone told me / I worked it out | **Realistic** |
| 9–11 | Updating on evidence (frequency-framed) | Change your mind by degree when counts shift | **Realistic with natural frequencies**; probability-% framing pushed too early |
| 10–12 | Base rates as "how common is it normally" | Don't ignore the background rate | **Realistic with concrete framing**; formal base-rate-neglect tasks are hard even for adults |
| 11–13 | Steelman before disagreeing | State the other view at its strongest first | **Realistic**; benefits from concrete examples not abstract rules |
| 11–14 | Judge a source (lateral reading) | Leave the page; check who's behind it & what others say | **Realistic**; validated from elementary up |
| 13–16 | Calibration as a measured habit | Track hit-rate vs stated confidence over time | **Realistic** |
| 14–18 | Evaluating AI output | Treat AI as a draft; "how would I check this?" | **Realistic to practise; evidence base thin** |
| 16–21 | Probabilistic forecasting + scoring | Make dated predictions, score with Brier, review | **Realistic late-adolescent** |

## 10–15 game-mechanic seeds

1. **Sort-and-Rule (decompose/classify).** Loop: drag items into bins by a hidden rule; deduce the rule. Scales: more attributes, exceptions, nested rules. Tracking: **mastery** (rule types unlocked).
2. **If-Then Machines (conditional logic).** Loop: build a chain of if-then gates to route a ball/character to a goal. Scales: AND/OR, NOT, then "valid but produces wrong output" puzzles (validity vs truth). Tracking: **mastery**.
3. **Cause Detective (cause vs correlation).** Loop: two things co-occur; design a tiny test (toggle one variable) to see which causes which. Scales: confounds, multiple causes. Tracking: mastery → **scored** on whether the test isolates the variable.
4. **One-Thing-At-A-Time (CVS).** Loop: a virtual pendulum/ramp; only fair tests (vary one variable) count. Scales: more variables. Tracking: **scored** (proportion of controlled experiments).
5. **Number-Bond Builder (CPA number sense).** Loop: compose/decompose numbers with blocks → pictures → symbols. Scales: bigger numbers, fractions. Tracking: **mastery**.
6. **Bar-Model Story Solver (Singapore).** Loop: turn a word problem into a bar model, then solve. Scales: multi-step, ratio, algebra-precursor. Tracking: **mastery**.
7. **Humane Fact Dojo (automaticity, anti-anxiety).** Loop: short daily spaced-retrieval bursts; you race *your own past self*, never a clock-shaming leaderboard; missed facts resurface sooner (spacing). Scales: adaptive item selection. Tracking: **mastery** (fact-fluency map), explicitly NOT a high-stakes timed score.
8. **Sure-O-Meter (calibration core).** Loop: answer, set confidence 1–5 (or a slider), see outcome, watch your personal calibration curve build. Scales: finer probability, more domains. Tracking: **psychometric/scored** (Brier).
9. **Prediction Diary (forecasting).** Loop: make dated real-world predictions ("Will it rain Saturday?"), resolve later, score. Scales: longer horizons, harder questions. Tracking: **scored** (Brier over time).
10. **Evidence Tipper (intuitive Bayes / base rates).** Loop: start with a "how common normally" jar of counts; new evidence adds/removes tokens; you read off the updated frequency. Scales: weaker/stronger evidence, two competing hypotheses. Tracking: mastery → scored.
11. **Steelman First (epistemic move).** Loop: before you can pick a side, you must select the strongest version of the *other* side; bonus for accuracy. Scales: subtler positions. Tracking: **mastery** (rubric).
12. **Source Sleuth / Lateral Reading.** Loop: given a claim + a web-style page, "open new tabs" to check who's behind it and what others say before judging. Scales: trickier sources. Tracking: **scored** (correct credibility judgement).
13. **Spot the Confident Robot (evaluate AI — EXPERIMENTAL).** Loop: an in-game "AI" answers questions, sometimes hallucinating confidently; player flags suspicious claims and chooses a check ("how would I verify this?"). Scales: subtler errors, domain spread. Tracking: **scored**, but flagged internally as unvalidated.
14. **Argument Map Studio.** Loop: drag claims/reasons/objections into a box-and-arrow map; the map "lights up" when structurally complete. Scales: rebuttals, co-premises. Tracking: **mastery**.
15. **Fallacy Zoo (implicit).** Loop: meet "creatures" embodying flawed moves (hasty generalisation, false cause, ad hominem) through *examples in context*, not definitions first. Scales: more types, mixed sets. Tracking: **mastery**; treat far-transfer as unproven.

## Calibration mechanic deep-dive (the highest-value epistemics build)

**Goal:** let the child bet/rate confidence and get honest, non-discouraging feedback.

**Elicitation by age:**
- Ages 7–9: 3 faces (sure / unsure / guessing).
- Ages 9–12: 1–5 stars.
- Ages 12+: a probability slider (50–100% for two-option questions; full 0–100% later).

**The scoring math — Brier score.** For a prediction with stated probability *p* on an event resolving to outcome *o* (1 if it happened, 0 if not): **Brier = (p − o)².** Lower is better; 0 is perfect, 0.25 is what you'd get always saying "50%," and 1.0 is confidently wrong. Average it over many predictions. Brier is a *proper scoring rule*: the score is mathematically minimised when you report your true belief, so it removes the incentive to over- or under-claim.

**How to present it to a child without discouragement:**
- **Never show a raw "you lost" per item.** Show a *calibration curve*: "When you said 4/5 sure, you were right 7 out of 10 times — so 4/5 was a bit too confident." Frame as a *map of self-knowledge*, not a grade.
- **Reward honesty explicitly.** Because Brier is proper, a kid who says "2/5" and is wrong should *feel fine*. Surface a separate "honesty/calibration" meter distinct from a "knowledge" meter, so being well-calibrated is a win even when wrong.
- **Use the "you'd get 0.25 by always guessing 50%" anchor** as the friendly baseline to beat — the child competes against chance, not against a humiliating zero.
- **Counter the known overconfidence baseline:** children (and weaker learners especially) start overconfident, so early sessions should make the over-confidence gap *visible and shrinkable* rather than punished. Design for gradual improvement — consistent with both adult calibration-training evidence and the child metacognition literature.
- For the youngest, skip numbers: a "confidence jar" that fills more when sure, and a post-reveal "was the jar right?" beat.

**Why this is the strongest epistemics bet:** calibration training has clear adult evidence (Good Judgment Project superforecasters are demonstrably well-calibrated; calibration improves with feedback), children's metacognitive monitoring measurably develops with age, and the feedback loop is concrete, gameable in the good sense, and entirely free of any CBT "challenge your thoughts" framing.

## Accessibility notes for a dyslexic 8-year-old

- **Audio-first, text-light.** Carry instructions and content via voice + symbols/icons; never gate a reasoning task behind reading load. The reasoning content (sorting, chance, confidence) is largely non-verbal — exploit that.
- **Symbols over text for core loops.** Use manipulatives, bars, jars, faces, and color rather than written prompts wherever possible (this also aligns with CPA and natural-frequency design).
- **Decouple reading skill from reasoning score.** A dyslexic strong reasoner must never be penalised on a logic/math/epistemics item because of decoding. Keep text short, high-contrast, generously spaced, left-aligned, sans-serif; offer text-to-speech on everything.
- **On special "dyslexia fonts":** treat claims for fonts like OpenDyslexic as **waarschijnlijk at best / likely overstated** — the stronger, safer evidence is for plain formatting choices (larger size, increased letter/line spacing, short lines, good contrast). Don't rely on a special font as a fix; rely on layout, spacing, and audio.
- **Chunk and checkpoint.** Short segments, frequent save points, no time pressure on reading (consistent with the humane-fluency stance).
- **Let speech be the input too.** Allow spoken answers / steelman statements rather than typed, where feasible.

## Open gaps & caveats (what the evidence does NOT support)

- **Far-transfer is the big honest caveat.** "Train logic/coding/critical-thinking and the child reasons better in general" is **not established.** Kassai et al. (2019), *Psychological Bulletin* 145(2): near-transfer g⁺ = 0.44 (k=43, p<.001, real), far-transfer g⁺ = 0.11 (k=17, p=.11, non-significant). Computational-thinking reviews (Scherer et al. 2019) show strong near-transfer, moderate-to-low far-transfer. Critical thinking is substantially **domain-specific** (Willingham). Design for the *specific* skills you want, repeated across many concrete domains; do not promise general "smarter thinking."
- **Q6 (evaluating AI output) is the thinnest area — flag honestly.** There is **no located empirical study showing children/teens get better at detecting AI hallucinations after instruction.** Recent reviews explicitly note the absence of detection frameworks and the under-representation of AI literacy in curricula. The closest descriptive work (a 2025 study of ~152 Chinese middle-schoolers) finds teens *overestimate* their AI literacy and over-trust chatbots — but measured trust, not detection skill. Even trained adults detect hallucinations poorly (one study reported ~55% accuracy overall, dropping to ~44% in complex cases) — so calibrate expectations for an 8-year-old accordingly. The transferable evidence is the lateral-reading lineage on *web* sources (see below); the AI-specific practitioner frameworks (Caulfield's "SIFT for AI," Nov 2025; UNESCO 2024; Common Sense Media; the Digital Inquiry Group/iCivics "Artificially Speaking: AI Chatbot Claims" lesson) carry **no efficacy data for the AI case.** Build the mechanic, but treat efficacy as an open research bet.
- **The lateral-reading evidence is real but not all RCT-grade — be precise.** McGrew & Breakstone (2023, *AERA Open* 9) was a **pretest/posttest** study (N=574, significant growth; the authors note random assignment was beyond its scope), NOT a cluster-randomised trial. A larger district-wide field study is Wineburg, Breakstone, McGrew, Smith & Ortega (2022), *Journal of Educational Psychology* 114(5):893–909. McGrew's (2024, *Current Opinion in Psychology*) review concludes interventions "from elementary school through adulthood have shown evidence of improving participants' digital evaluations." Net: lateral reading improving *web-source* judgement is **zeker**; transfer to AI output is plausible but **unproven**.
- **Boaler's specific claims are contested.** The pedagogy (number sense, conceptual depth, reducing high-stakes speed pressure) is reasonable and broadly consistent with good practice, but specific "timed tests cause math anxiety / neuroscience proves X" claims have been challenged as mis-citing their sources (a formal complaint at Stanford alleges misrepresentation; one cited study reportedly did no brain imaging). Simultaneously, automaticity via spaced retrieval is well-supported (and natural-frequency/format effects are quantitatively robust — McDowell & Jacobs 2017, *Psychological Bulletin* 143). **Judgement call:** adopt humane fluency-building (spaced, low-stakes, self-referenced) — neither high-stakes timed drills nor a no-practice stance.
- **Argument-mapping evidence** is promising but skews to adults/undergraduates and includes uncontrolled studies (e.g., van Gelder et al. 2004 lacked a treated control); its value for a child is a reasonable bet, not proven.
- **Calibration in children specifically** is supported by the metacognition literature (monitoring improves with age; overconfidence is the default — Finn & Metcalfe 2014; Bayard et al. 2021) and by strong adult training evidence, but a clean "calibration training in young children improves calibration" RCT is not something to cite with confidence — treat child calibration gains as **waarschijnlijk**.
- **Fallacy instruction transfer is weak;** teach implicitly through examples, and beware the trap that "knowing fallacy names = thinking well."
