# The Inner & Relational Layer: A Research-Grounded Design Brief
## A long-horizon educational game for a Dutch, dyslexic boy, ages 7→21 — IFS · NVC · Interoception

## TL;DR
- **Build the game on IFS "parts," NVC feelings/needs, and bodily-awareness regulation because they are humane, coherent, and teachable — but with eyes open: the evidence for IFS, polyvagal theory, branded regulation curricula (Zones of Regulation), and much of SEL is THIN or CONTESTED, not "zeker."** The single best-evidenced building block is *emotional granularity / labeling feelings* (Lisa Feldman Barrett; Daniel Siegel's "name it to tame it"), and the strongest *structural* finding is that interoception is correlated with emotion regulation in healthy children.
- **The honest dividing line: this is skill-building and self-knowledge for a healthy child, NOT therapy.** The game may teach a child to notice a part with curiosity, name a body sensation, and say a true thing without blame. It must NEVER attempt to work with trauma, "exiles," dissociation, or wounded parts — those are handed to a human professional. Tracking stays at "Model B at its lightest": presence/streak of *whether* reflection happened, never a rating of feelings or character.
- **CBT is excluded by request** (listed explicitly in the framework menu) and **positive-thinking/affirmations are not the mechanism.** The mechanism is bodily awareness + turning toward one's parts with curiosity + clean expression — and the parent's co-regulation, which is the most evidence-backed lever of all in the early rungs.

---

## Key Findings

1. **IFS is "true and humane" but not strongly evidenced — and that's fine if used honestly.** IFS (Richard Schwartz) rests on one oft-cited proof-of-concept RCT — Shadick et al. 2013 (*J Rheumatology* 40(11):1831–1841), which "randomized [patients] to either an IFS group for 9 months (n=39) or an education (control) group (n=40)," total n=79 — which got IFS onto SAMHSA's NREPP registry in 2015, plus a small depression study: Haddock et al. 2016/2017 (*J Marital & Family Therapy* 43(1):131–144), in which "college women (N=37) were randomly allocated to IFS treatment or treatment as usual (CBT or IPT)" (IFS n=22 vs TAU n=15) and which found "a decline in depressive symptoms for both conditions and no significant differences." Critics note small samples, few independent replications, and conflict-of-interest concerns. Evidence flag: **THIN but promising; non-clinical parent-led "notice your parts" use is low-risk.** There IS a dedicated practitioner literature for children (Spiegel & Stubbs, *Internal Family Systems Therapy with Children*; the IFS Institute's child-counseling materials) describing kid-friendly methods: externalizing parts, "direct access," and ground rules ("all parts welcome, but no hurting/breaking").

2. **Emotional granularity is the best-evidenced inner skill.** Lisa Feldman Barrett's work shows people who construct finer-grained emotion concepts regulate better; vocabulary "seeds" the concepts. This is "waarschijnlijk" solid and maps directly onto a Dutch/dyslexic child via spoken, illustrated, body-anchored words rather than text.

3. **Interoception → emotion regulation is a real link in healthy children, but training curricula are weakly evidenced.** The correlational evidence (Hechler/Schaan group, typically-developing preschoolers — e.g., Schaan et al. 2019, *Int. J. Psychophysiology* 138:47–56; Opdensteinen et al. 2021) is **MODERATE**. The *intervention* evidence (Kelly Mahler's Interoception Curriculum — Mahler et al. 2022, *Occupational Therapy International*, n=14 autistic students, uncontrolled pre/post; Mahler et al. 2024 special-ed feasibility) is **THIN** — small, uncontrolled feasibility studies in autistic/special-ed samples, with the developer disclosing she "receives income from their sales." Use interoception as a rationale and an activity, not as a guaranteed outcome.

4. **Polyvagal theory's neuroanatomy is scientifically disputed — keep regulation simple.** Grossman (2023) and Grossman et al. 2026, "The Polyvagal Theory Is Untenable: An International Expert Evaluation" (*Clinical Neuropsychiatry*) — which Porges characterizes as Grossman plus a "self-selection of 38 co-authors" (39 total) — argue the core physiological premises (RSA as a vagal-tone proxy, the asocial-reptile/social-mammal evolutionary story, the ventral/dorsal vagal architecture) are untenable; Porges rebuts this in the same issue ("When a critique becomes untenable"). The user's instinct is right: teach **bodily awareness + turning toward parts with curiosity**, framed as felt states (calm/alert/overwhelmed) and down-regulation (breath, movement, rest) — NOT as polyvagal neuroscience.

5. **NVC is humane and widely used but lightly evidenced; "self-empathy → honest expression" is the unifying loop the user intuited.** Marshall Rosenberg's observation→feeling→need→request model has thin controlled-efficacy data (some studies suggest increased empathy, decreased anger). Crucially, NVC *already contains* the inner+outer loop via "self-empathy" (inner noticing) → "honest expression" (blame-free outward speech). There is a credible practitioner literature *crossing* Focusing (Gendlin) with NVC — e.g., Romeo-Biedma 2014, "Crossing Focusing and Nonviolent Communication," *The Folio* — and IFS with interpersonal communication (Herbine-Blank's "U-Turn" in *Intimacy from the Inside Out*; Somatic IFS by McConnell). All of this is **practitioner/theoretical, not trialed in children.**

6. **Mindfulness-for-kids is overclaimed; the big trial was null and sometimes harmful.** The MYRIAD trial randomised 85 schools (8,376 students, ages 11–14, mean 12.2) 1:1 to the ".b" programme vs teaching-as-usual; Kuyken et al. 2022 (*Evid Based Ment Health*, CC BY) reported "no significant effects of MBIs on several mental health and well-being outcomes," with signals of possible worsening for at-risk youth and younger adolescents struggling to apply the skills. Implication: don't make seated mindfulness the spine; brief body-anchored noticing is fine, universal meditation drills are not.

7. **Attention is partly trainable but mostly developmental; protecting it from engineered distraction is the higher-leverage move.** Sustained attention develops fast to ~age 10 then plateaus. Media multitasking is reliably associated with self-reported attention problems (though objective-performance links are weaker/mixed, and some screen-time/attention studies find no linear effect). The defensible game design: single-tasking, focus reps tied to interest, and explicit "attention protection" literacy.

8. **Co-regulation is the most solid early lever — and it runs through the parent.** Rosanbalm & Murray's widely used practice brief (US ACF/OPRE, public-domain government document) defines co-regulation as warm relationship + coaching/modeling + structured environment, adapted by age. This is the place the game should most deliberately invite the parent in.

## Details

### 1. IFS for children — developmental & practical picture
IFS holds that the mind is naturally multiple ("parts," each with good intentions) led by a calm, curious **Self** (the "8 C's"). Protectors (managers, firefighters) guard **exiles** (wounded parts). For a healthy child's game, only the *front door* of this model is appropriate: noticing "a part of me feels X," turning toward it with curiosity, and learning all parts are welcome.

- **At 8:** parts are concrete and externalized ("the worried part feels like a knot"). Children accept parts easily but manage them poorly (Johnson & Schwartz). Use characters/animals, drawing, naming.
- **At 12:** can hold ambivalence ("part of me wants to, part of me is scared"), beginning meta-cognition.
- **At 16:** can reflect on patterns over time and on relationships between parts, and on Self-leadership as a stance.

**The line a game must not cross:** Any work *with* exiles, trauma memories, dissociation, "unburdening," or a part that wants to harm self/others. The game stays at recognition + curiosity + (optional) talking to a trusted human. Schwartz's own "direct access" and "unburdening" methods are therapy and belong to a professional.

### 2. Emotional literacy & interoception (ages ~7–21)
- **Granularity (Barrett):** expand the felt vocabulary — not "bad" but "jittery / heavy / prickly / hollow." Words seed concepts; concepts sharpen regulation. **Best-evidenced.**
- **"Name it to tame it" (Siegel & Bryson, *The Whole-Brain Child*):** naming + brief storytelling of an upset. Popular and useful, but note the honest critique: the catchphrase outruns its neuroscience, and some critics argue it over-privileges verbal/logical "taming" over simply *being with* a sensation. Treat as **plausible, not proven**; pair it with felt-sense work so it doesn't become "explain your feeling away."
- **Felt sense (Gendlin's Focusing):** attending to the vague bodily "something" before words; finding the word that "fits." Adapted for children via drawing, movement, metaphor (International Focusing Institute materials). Fits a dyslexic child well because it is pre-verbal and multimodal.
- **Interoception activities:** hunger "battery," warm-mug shoulder-drop, sensation Simon-Says, body-mapping. Build the link sensation→emotion. **Rationale is moderate; specific curricula are thin.**
- **What is fluff:** generic "emotion of the day" charts with no body anchor and no granularity; anything that becomes a rating scale of the child.

### 3. Nervous-system / co-regulation basics
Teach three felt states — **calm / alert / overwhelmed** — and three down-regulation families: **breath, movement, rest.** Keep the model phenomenological (what it feels like in the body) not neuroanatomical. **Do not teach polyvagal theory as fact** — its physiological claims are contested (Grossman et al.; neuroanatomists dispute the claimed brainstem connections). The branded "Zones of Regulation" (color-coded states) is popular but, per Mason, Leaf & Gerhardt 2024 (*J Special Education* 57(4):219–229), "the Zones of Regulation does not meet the standards for an evidence-based practice" against APA, National Standards Project, and National Clearinghouse of Autism Evidence and Practice criteria. You can borrow the *idea* of named states without buying the proprietary system or its claims.

**Co-regulation (the parent as mechanism):** the most solid finding in this whole brief. Before a child can self-regulate, a calm adult regulates *with* them. The game should script invitations for the parent to be present, model, and structure the environment — not to grade the child.

### 4. Communication — NVC and beyond
- **NVC (Rosenberg):** observation (no evaluation) → feeling → need → request (not demand). For a child: "When ___ happened (just the facts), I felt ___, because I need ___. Would you be willing to ___?"
- **At 8:** one concrete observation + one feeling word + one simple request; heavy scaffolding, parent models.
- **At 15:** full four-step, distinguishing feelings from thoughts/judgments, requests from demands, and handling another's anger as "an unmet need," not an attack.
- **Active/reflective listening (Rogers lineage):** paraphrase + check; modest but real evidence it raises felt-understanding and satisfaction (effect sizes small but positive in initial-interaction studies).
- **Theory of mind:** false-belief ~age 4; but "advanced ToM"/recursive perspective-taking keeps developing into late adolescence/early adulthood (Valle et al. 2015 found young adults outperform adolescents on third-order false-belief tasks) — so perspective-taking challenges should scale in recursion (what *he* thinks *she* thinks) with age.
- **Well-evidenced vs idealistic:** that listening and clean "I-statements" reduce conflict is reasonably supported; that NVC reliably transforms relationships is **idealistic/under-evidenced** — present it as a skill that helps, not a guarantee.

### 5. The shared tension loop
The user's loop — **notice a tension (which PART feels it) → articulate it cleanly (NVC, no blame) → make a request/proposal** — is real and has named practitioner support:
- NVC's own **self-empathy → honest expression** is exactly this loop inside one framework.
- **Focusing × NVC** "crossing" (Romeo-Biedma, *The Folio*; practitioners Allan Rohlfs, Kathy McGuire): felt sense feeds clean expression; both traditions descend from the Rogers/humanistic lineage.
- **IFS in relationships:** Herbine-Blank's "U-Turn" (turn inward to your own reactive part *before* responding) and Somatic IFS (McConnell) anchor parts in the body.

Scaffold for a child: (1) body check — *where do I feel it?* (2) which part is this — *name it, curious not critical*; (3) the true sentence — *fact + feeling + need + ask*. **Caveat: this integration is practitioner wisdom, not trialed in children.**

### 6. Attention as trainable
- Sustained attention rises steeply to ~age 10, then plateaus (developmental, not mainly trainable by drills).
- Single-tasking, novelty, hands-on tasks, and *interest* extend focus.
- **Digital distraction:** media multitasking is consistently linked to self-reported attention problems and task delay; the mere presence of a phone can degrade a child's task-related brain activity. Objective-performance effects are smaller/mixed and some screen-time/attention studies (e.g., 6–10-year-olds) find no linear link — so the honest message is "engineered interruption fragments focus," not "screens rot your brain." Teach attention *protection* (notifications off, one thing at a time) as a felt experiment.
- **Overclaimed:** brain-training games' transfer; universal school mindfulness for attention (MYRIAD null).

### 7. Physical-health basics (SMALL — needs more research later)
Frame as **fuel for the things he wants to do**, never weight/calories, never shame. Highest-leverage, best-evidenced:
- **Sleep:** consistent bed/wake times; protect from nighttime screens (linked to lower daytime attention and school performance).
- **Movement:** daily moderate-to-vigorous activity (WHO frames ~60 min/day for school-age children); movement also supports sleep and mood.
- **Food as fuel:** regular meals incl. breakfast; notice how the body *feels* before/after (interoception tie-in) rather than rules.
> **This section is deliberately minimal and flagged as needing dedicated later research** (esp. adolescent nutrition without disordered-eating risk; the user should commission a separate evidence pass).

## 10–15 GAME-MECHANIC SEEDS (light, mostly private/diegetic)
Each: **skill · loop · difficulty scaling · tracking note (minimal).**

1. **The Ranger Meets His Fear Before the Storm.** Skill: notice a part with curiosity. Loop: alone at the cabin, a "scared part" appears as a small creature; player chooses to greet it, ask what it wants, and let it ride along. Scaling: 8 = name it; 14 = ask its good intention; 18 = lead from calm. Tracking: a lantern lights to show *that* he paused — never how scared he was.
2. **The Angry Farmer (NVC choice).** Skill: clean expression under heat. Loop: farmer accuses you; options range from blame to a fact+feeling+need+ask line. Scaling: more nuance/recursion with age. Tracking: notes *whether* he tried a non-blaming line, not its "quality."
3. **Body Weather Vane.** Skill: interoception. Loop: optional check before a big choice — calm/alert/overwhelmed as wind/sky. Scaling: more sensation words unlock. Tracking: a streak of check-ins, no values.
4. **The Two-Voices Bridge.** Skill: holding ambivalence. Loop: two parts argue at a fork; player lets both speak before choosing. Tracking: presence of "let both speak," not which won.
5. **Felt-Sense Sketchbook.** Skill: Gendlin felt sense. Loop: draw "where it sits in the body"; pick the word that fits. Dyslexia-friendly (drawing > text). Tracking: count of entries only.
6. **The U-Turn Gate.** Skill: check own part before reacting. Loop: before a heated reply, a mirror offers a 2-second inward glance. Scaling: appears later (13+). Tracking: a quiet chime, no log of content.
7. **Down-Shift Toolkit.** Skill: regulation. Loop: discover breath/movement/rest "tools," learn which calms *this* body. Tracking: which tools were *tried*, never a calm-score.
8. **Listening Lantern.** Skill: reflective listening. Loop: paraphrase an NPC's worry to unlock the real conversation. Tracking: presence of paraphrase step.
9. **The Quiet Part.** Skill: notice a protector pattern. Loop: a companion who goes silent under stress; over chapters, player gets curious about why. Tracking: long-arc, presence-only.
10. **Attention Campfire.** Skill: single-tasking. Loop: tend one fire vs. chasing sparks (notifications); feel focus build. Tracking: longest single-task streak, framed as the *fire*, not the child.
11. **Steelman the Stranger.** Skill: recursive perspective-taking. Loop: state the other character's view so well they feel understood. Scaling: deeper recursion with age. Tracking: completion only.
12. **Repair the Rope Bridge.** Skill: conflict repair. Loop: after a rupture with an ally, choose to re-open, own your part, restore. Tracking: *that* repair was attempted.
13. **Fuel Garden (health).** Skill: sleep/movement/food as fuel. Loop: rest and eat to have energy for the quest; notice the felt difference. Tracking: habit *presence*, never weight/calorie anything.
14. **The Curiosity Compass.** Skill: Self-energy stance. Loop: a compass that points "toward, with curiosity" whenever a part shows up. Tracking: times the compass was *used*.

> **Governing tracking rule across all seeds:** *Oordelen zijn voor het getal, niet voor het kind.* Track at most presence/streak of reflection. Never rate feelings, character, "calmness," or "kindness." Reflections are private and diegetic by default.

## NEVER CROSS THIS LINE (and how to hand off)
A game must **not** attempt, and should gracefully hand to a human:
- **Trauma, abuse, exiles, "unburdening," dissociation, flashbacks.** If a "part" reveals real pain/memory, the mechanic should gently say: *"This sounds important and big — this is for a trusted person, not the game,"* and surface a "talk to your parent / a grown-up you trust" card.
- **Self-harm, suicidal thoughts, harm to others.** Hard stop → caregiver + professional resources; never a game choice.
- **Diagnosis or anything that rates the child** (anxiety scores, "how dysregulated are you").
- **Coercive emotion ("calm down now," forced gratitude/affirmations).**
- **Polyvagal/neuro claims presented as established fact.**
- **Pressure to disclose feelings.** Reflection is always optional and private.

Hand-off design: a recurring, non-alarming **"This is a people-thing, not a game-thing"** motif so escalation feels normal, not like failure.

## CO-REGULATION SECTION (where the PARENT is the mechanism)
Co-regulation (Rosanbalm & Murray) = warm relationship + coaching/modeling + structured, predictable environment, adapted by age. It precedes and enables self-regulation, especially in the early rungs. The game invites it by:
- **Two-player "with-me" moments:** prompts like *"Find your grown-up and do the breath together"* before a hard scene — the parent co-does, doesn't grade.
- **Parent-as-model cards:** the game occasionally asks the parent to share *their own* part/feeling out loud (modeling, not performing).
- **Repair scripts for the parent:** short, non-expert prompts for re-connecting after a rough moment (own your part, no lecture).
- **A myth-buster note for parents:** co-regulation does NOT mean hovering through every storm; it scales down as the child grows. Over-attachment to "always be beside them" can backfire and inhibit self-regulation.
- **Environment, not exhortation:** the game nudges predictable routines (sleep, one-thing-at-a-time) as the family's shared scaffolding.

## ACCESSIBILITY (dyslexia; emotional content read-aloud)
- **Read-aloud everything, especially emotional content;** never gate inner/relational learning behind reading fluency. High-quality TTS + human voice-over for key feeling-words.
- **Picture-first feeling vocabulary;** sensations as images/animations (knot, spark, fog) before words.
- **Multimodal felt sense:** drawing, movement, color, sound — leverages Gendlin's non-verbal strength and suits dyslexia.
- **Dyslexia-friendly type** (generous spacing, sans-serif, high contrast, short lines), but treat type as secondary to audio.
- **No timed reading;** no spelling/typing required to express a feeling — choose from spoken/illustrated options.
- **Consistent icons** for the three states and the four NVC steps so the *structure* is learnable without reading.

## OPEN GAPS / CAVEATS
- **Population mismatch:** the branded curricula (Zones, Mahler interoception) were tested mostly on autistic/special-ed children, not healthy kids — extrapolation is not directly evidence-supported.
- **Conflict of interest** taints several regulation sources (developers studying their own products — explicitly so for Mahler; and Zones' strongest "evidence" comes from its vendor site and dissertations).
- **No RCT** of an interoception curriculum, or of a combined IFS+NVC protocol, in typically-developing children.
- **IFS, polyvagal, NVC, much of SEL = THIN/CONTESTED.** The Durlak et al. 2011 SEL meta-analysis (*Child Development* 82(1):405–432; 213 programs, 270,034 K–12 students) found an academic-performance effect size of g=0.27, "which translates into a percentile difference of 11%," plus SEL-skills g=0.57, attitudes g=0.23, conduct problems g=0.22, emotional distress g=0.24 — modest, heterogeneous, and implementation-dependent (Cipriano et al. 2023 reaffirms small-to-moderate effects).
- **Long-horizon validity:** none of this is validated as a 7→21 spiral; treat the spiral as a humane design hypothesis, reviewed as the child grows.
- **Physical-health section is intentionally tiny and under-researched** — commission a dedicated pass.
- **Dutch application from English sources:** terminology/feeling-words must be re-grounded in Dutch with a native check; do not machine-translate the emotion vocabulary.

## Recommendations (staged, with thresholds that change them)

**Stage 0 — Foundations (build first, lowest risk, best evidence).**
1. Ship the **emotional-granularity vocabulary** and **co-regulation invitations** first — these are the best-supported elements. Make every feeling-word audio + picture.
2. Lock the **tracking rule** ("presence/streak only, never a rating") into the engine as a hard constraint, not a guideline. *Threshold to revisit:* never — this is a values commitment, not an empirical one.
3. Implement the **"people-thing, not a game-thing" hand-off** before any parts-work ships.

**Stage 1 — Inner + relational core (ages 7–12 content).**
4. Build IFS as **front-door only** (notice/name/curiosity) and NVC as **scaffolded fact+feeling+need+ask**, with the parent as model.
5. Add interoception as **felt experiments** (body weather, hunger battery), framed as rationale not promise.
6. Keep regulation **phenomenological** (calm/alert/overwhelmed; breath/movement/rest). Do not encode polyvagal or Zones claims.

**Stage 2 — Adolescent depth (13–21).**
7. Introduce the **tension loop** (body check → which part → clean sentence), the **U-Turn**, recursive perspective-taking, and conflict repair.
8. Add **attention-protection literacy** as felt before/after experiments.

**What would change the plan (benchmarks):**
- If a **well-powered RCT in healthy children** shows an interoception or parts-based curriculum works (or fails), upgrade/downgrade that strand accordingly.
- If user testing shows **any child distress or a part surfacing real pain**, tighten the hand-off and reduce depth — this is the trip-wire that moves a mechanic from "game" to "professional."
- If **engagement collapses on text**, push further into audio/visual/drawing (the dyslexia lever).
- Commission the **dedicated physical-health and Dutch-localization passes** before those sections go live.
